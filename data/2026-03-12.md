## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Today's cultural landscape reveals a profound tension between authenticity and accessibility, as established institutions grapple with relevance while emerging voices demand recognition on their own terms. The drinks industry faces a reckoning: Champagne's three-year decline to 20-year lows signals that premiumisation without purpose is failing, while Cambodia's unregulated beer market exposes the dark underbelly of aggressive alcohol capitalism in developing economies. These aren't isolated data points—they're bookends of an industry questioning its social contract.

The most significant cultural current running through today's signals is the democratisation of expertise and experience. From the World Atlas of Wine's ninth edition attempting to remain authoritative in an age of TikTok sommeliers, to Canadian MPs fighting archaic laws blocking direct-to-consumer wine shipments, gatekeepers are being challenged at every level. Meanwhile, identity-based communities are crystallising around specific activities and values—whether anglers bonding over Busch Light collector cans or transmasculine ballroom scenes in Rio creating their own beauty standards.

For non-alcoholic beverage strategists, the opportunity lies in the spaces between these tensions. The Midwest American dining renaissance documented across Indianapolis, St. Louis, and Dayton reveals consumers hungry for unpretentious excellence—food and drink that tells authentic stories without demanding reverence. The success of immigrant-owned restaurants bringing personal narratives to the table suggests that provenance and personality trump polish.

The health equity conversation is also shifting, with NHS approvals for menopause treatments and critiques of "male by default" clinical guidelines signalling that functional benefits must be genuinely inclusive, not performatively so. Brands that understand these nuances—serving real needs for real communities—will capture the cultural moment. Those chasing trends without substance will join Champagne's declining shipment figures.

## TREND SIGNALS

### SIGNAL 1: The Champagne Correction

**What happened:** Champagne shipments hit 266 million bottles in 2025—the lowest in 20 years (excluding pandemic 2020)—down from a post-pandemic high of 326 million in 2022. Industry leaders express cautious pessimism, with Pol Roger's president suggesting the region should be "pleased" to match 2025 levels.

**Why it is a signal:** This represents the end of reflexive luxury consumption. The 55-million-bottle drop in two years suggests consumers are questioning whether celebration requires Champagne, or whether Champagne's "desirability" narrative has become disconnected from actual desire.

**Societal layer:** Status / pleasure

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Premium NA sparkling wines have a genuine window to capture celebration occasions. The opportunity isn't to mimic Champagne's positioning but to offer a new narrative around meaningful moments that doesn't rely on alcohol as the signifier of importance.

**What to monitor:** Sparkling wine sales by occasion type; emergence of "celebration" as a distinct beverage category separate from alcohol content; hospitality venues creating signature toasting rituals with NA options.

### SIGNAL 2: Neo-Colonial Alcohol Capitalism Exposed

**What happened:** A new documentary, *Cambodian Beer Dreams*, exposes how Cambodia's unregulated beer industry—with no legal drinking age and aggressive marketing through "beer girls"—has driven a fivefold increase in alcohol consumption, with 1 in 10 deaths now attributed to alcohol.

**Why it is a signal:** This represents growing global consciousness about alcohol's systemic harms, particularly in markets where regulation hasn't kept pace with corporate expansion. The "Wild East" framing positions certain alcohol industry practices as exploitative rather than celebratory.

**Societal layer:** Control / community

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** NA brands have an opportunity to position themselves as ethical alternatives in emerging markets, but must avoid replicating the same aggressive marketing tactics. Authentic community benefit—not just harm reduction messaging—will differentiate responsible players.

**What to monitor:** Regulatory developments in Southeast Asian markets; NGO partnerships in beverage sector; consumer sentiment research on alcohol industry ethics in developing economies.

### SIGNAL 3: The Midwest Dining Renaissance

**What happened:** Eater's comprehensive guides to Indianapolis, St. Louis, Dayton, and Oklahoma City reveal thriving food scenes defined by immigrant-owned restaurants, unpretentious fine dining, and personal storytelling—challenging coastal culinary hegemony.

**Why it is a signal:** The consistent theme across these cities is authenticity without pretension. Restaurants succeed by bringing "personal stories to the table" rather than chasing trends. This represents a broader rejection of performative sophistication in favour of genuine connection.

**Societal layer:** Belonging / identity

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** NA beverage brands should study how these restaurants build loyalty through narrative authenticity. The opportunity is in regional partnerships with establishments that value story over status, creating drinks menus that reflect local identity rather than imported sophistication.

**What to monitor:** James Beard nominations in secondary markets; social media engagement patterns for "flyover" food content; beverage pairing innovations at immigrant-owned establishments.

### SIGNAL 5: Democratising Wine Access

**What happened:** Canadian MP Dan Albas introduced a bill to amend the Canada Post Corporation Act, removing federal restrictions on direct-to-consumer alcohol shipments between provinces. The move is framed around self-reliance: "We need to be our own best customers."

**Why it is a signal:** This represents the ongoing dismantling of gatekeeping structures in beverage distribution. The framing around supporting "small producers" and "artisan" makers suggests consumers increasingly expect direct relationships with makers.

**Societal layer:** Control / community

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** NA brands should invest in direct-to-consumer infrastructure and storytelling that creates intimacy with makers. As regulatory barriers fall for alcohol, NA brands have an opportunity to lead in building direct relationships that bypass traditional retail gatekeepers.

**What to monitor:** DTC legislation changes across markets; subscription model adoption in beverage categories; consumer research on maker-relationship preferences.

### SIGNAL 6: Functional Health Equity

**What happened:** The NHS approved Veoza (fezolinetant) for hot flush prevention, while separate research revealed women receive worse treatment for back pain due to "male by default" clinical guidelines that ignore sex-based differences.

**Why it is a signal:** Health solutions are being scrutinised for genuine inclusivity rather than assumed universality. The critique of "male by default" approaches suggests consumers will increasingly demand functional products designed for their specific biology, not adapted from a presumed norm.

**Societal layer:** Identity / mental health

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Functional NA beverages must move beyond generic wellness claims to address specific, researched needs for distinct populations. Products designed explicitly for women's health, hormonal cycles, or other specific conditions will outperform generic "wellness" positioning.

**What to monitor:** Clinical research on sex-specific responses to functional ingredients; consumer demand for personalised functional beverages; regulatory guidance on health claims for specific populations.

### SIGNAL 7: The Collector Economy Intensifies

**What happened:** Two casks of whisky from "ghost" Japanese distillery Karuizawa sold for £4.25 million at Christie's—the highest value lots ever sold at a Christie's Wine and Spirit auction. Chilean winery VIK released a rare vertical of flagship wines as a collector's set.

**Why it is a signal:** Scarcity and provenance continue to drive extraordinary premiums in beverage collecting. The "ghost distillery" narrative—products from places that no longer exist—creates irreplaceable value that transcends the liquid itself.

**Societal layer:** Status / pleasure

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** NA brands should consider limited-edition strategies that create genuine scarcity and collectibility. The opportunity isn't in mimicking alcohol's collector culture but in establishing NA-specific markers of rarity—perhaps tied to seasonal ingredients, maker collaborations, or process innovations.

**What to monitor:** Secondary market activity for limited NA releases; collector community formation around NA categories; auction house interest in NA beverages.

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** NA brands should consider how to build authoritative voices in a category without established canons. The opportunity is in creating educational content and credentialing systems that establish expertise without the gatekeeping baggage of traditional wine culture.

## TOP 3 EXPERIMENTS

### Experiment 1: The Angler's Aperitif

**Hypothesis:** Identity-anchored communities with strong rituals (like fishing) will adopt NA beverages if positioned as enhancing rather than replacing their existing practices, particularly when endorsed by authentic community voices.

**Method:** Partner with three regional fishing guides or outdoor content creators to co-develop a limited-edition NA beverage designed for "on the water" consumption—emphasising hydration, refreshment, and flavour profiles that complement outdoor settings. Create collector packaging with regional fishing imagery. Launch through fishing-specific retail channels (bait shops, marina stores) and direct-to-consumer with community-exclusive early access.

**Success metric:** 40% of purchasers self-identify as "regular anglers" in post-purchase survey; 25% repeat purchase rate within 90 days; organic social sharing rate of 15% (purchasers posting product in fishing contexts).

---

### Experiment 2: The Midwest Story Series

**Hypothesis:** Consumers in secondary markets respond more strongly to beverages with authentic local narratives than to imported premium positioning, and will pay premium prices for products that reflect their regional identity.

**Method:** Identify three immigrant-owned restaurants in Indianapolis, St. Louis, or Oklahoma City with strong local followings. Collaborate with each to create a co-branded NA beverage inspired by their culinary heritage and personal story. Document the creation process through short-form video content. Sell exclusively through the partner restaurants and a limited DTC release, with proceeds supporting local immigrant business associations.

**Success metric:** Restaurant partners report NA beverage sales representing 8%+ of total beverage revenue; video content achieves 50% higher engagement than brand average; 60% of DTC purchasers are from the partner restaurant's metro area.

---

### Experiment 3: The Functional Specificity Test

**Hypothesis:** Functional NA beverages designed explicitly for women's hormonal health (with transparent research and specific claims) will outperform generic "wellness" positioning in both conversion and loyalty metrics.

**Method:** Develop two versions of a functional NA beverage with identical ingredients: Version A with generic wellness messaging ("supports balance and vitality") and Version B with specific women's health messaging ("formulated for hormonal wellness during perimenopause"). Run parallel DTC campaigns to women 40-55 with identical creative except for positioning. Track conversion, repeat purchase, and qualitative feedback on trust and relevance.

**Success metric:** Version B achieves 30%+ higher conversion rate; Version B customers show 40%+ higher repeat purchase rate at 60 days; qualitative feedback indicates Version B messaging feels "more honest" or "more relevant" at 2:1 ratio vs. Version A.
