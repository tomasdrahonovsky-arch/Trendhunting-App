## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Today's cultural landscape reveals a fundamental tension between authenticity and performance, between heritage and innovation, and between individual wellness and collective experience. The drinks industry is navigating this through strategic repositioning: legacy players are acquiring trend-spotting capabilities (Terold/Off-Piste), while heritage brands seek cultural legitimacy through experiential activations (Bijou at Sound Bites) and governmental recognition (Uruguay's wine-as-living-culture declaration).

The non-alcoholic and functional beverage space continues its maturation. Henkell Freixenet's flat overall sales mask genuine growth in no-alcohol sparkling wines, while Damm's Daura 0.0% launch explicitly targets the underserved intersection of gluten-free and alcohol-free—a niche-within-a-niche strategy that signals market sophistication. Meanwhile, Modelo's simultaneous launch of both high-ABV (8%) and non-alcoholic variants reveals a bifurcated consumer: the same demographic seeking "bolder, higher-proof experiences" is also driving moderation trends.

A critical insight emerges from the Modelo positioning: Gen Z and Millennials are nostalgic for pre-digital social rituals—"getting ready for a night out" moments from the early 2010s. This represents a profound shift: the generation raised on smartphones craves the analogue anticipation their older siblings experienced. For beverage brands, this opens territory around ritual, preparation, and shared pre-experience moments rather than just consumption occasions.

The health-taxation conversation in Europe (calls for higher alcohol and junk food taxes to cover liver disease costs) signals regulatory headwinds that will accelerate premiumisation in alcohol while creating tailwinds for sophisticated non-alcoholic alternatives. Brands that can deliver genuine complexity, cultural credibility, and social currency without alcohol are positioned for structural advantage.

## TREND SIGNALS

### SIGNAL 1: The Acquisition of Trend-Spotting Capability

**What happened:** Terold Invest acquired UK wholesaler Off-Piste Wines specifically for its ability to "spot and scale trends ahead of the market," calling the UK "a critical engine for innovation and category leadership."

**Why it is a signal:** Major global players are acknowledging they cannot innovate internally fast enough. They're acquiring cultural antennae rather than building them—a tacit admission that proximity to emerging consumer behaviour is now a strategic asset worth paying premium prices for.

**Societal layer:** Control

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Non-alcoholic brands with demonstrated trend-identification capabilities become acquisition targets. Building visible cultural intelligence infrastructure (content, community, data) increases enterprise value beyond product sales.

**What to monitor:** M&A activity targeting small, culturally-connected distributors and brand incubators; job postings for "cultural insight" roles at major beverage companies.

---

### SIGNAL 2: Nostalgia for Pre-Digital Social Rituals

**What happened:** Modelo's marketing explicitly targets Gen Z and Millennial nostalgia for "getting ready for a night out" rituals from the early 2010s, positioning their new high-ABV product for "drinking occasions before a night out."

**Why it is a signal:** The smartphone-native generation is experiencing nostalgia for experiences they barely had—the anticipatory, communal preparation rituals that preceded social media documentation. This represents a desire for process over outcome, anticipation over arrival.

**Societal layer:** Belonging / Community

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Massive opportunity in the "pre-game" occasion with sophisticated non-alcoholic options. Products positioned for preparation rituals (getting ready drinks, pre-dinner aperitifs) rather than the main event itself. Packaging and serving rituals become as important as liquid quality.

**What to monitor:** TikTok content around "getting ready" rituals; growth in pre-mixed aperitivo-style drinks; emergence of "preparation" as a distinct consumption occasion in market research.

---

### SIGNAL 3: The Gluten-Free/Alcohol-Free Intersection

**What happened:** Damm launched Daura 0.0%, explicitly targeting the "scarce" UK market combination of gluten-free and alcohol-free beer, citing 500,000 undiagnosed coeliac sufferers and younger drinkers who "feel better without" gluten.

**Why it is a signal:** Functional stacking is becoming standard. Consumers increasingly expect products to solve multiple concerns simultaneously. The "feel better without" framing extends beyond medical necessity to lifestyle optimisation—gluten avoidance as wellness signal rather than medical requirement.

**Societal layer:** Identity / Mental Health

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Multi-functional positioning becomes table stakes. Non-alcoholic beverages should consider additional functional claims (gut health, anti-inflammatory, adaptogenic) as competitive differentiation. Menu descriptions should communicate multiple benefits.

**What to monitor:** Growth in "free-from" stacking (gluten-free + sugar-free + alcohol-free); consumer research on functional benefit prioritisation; emergence of "clean label" as primary purchase driver.

---

### SIGNAL 4: Wine Seeks Cultural Legitimacy Through Experience

**What happened:** Bijou wine partnered with Sound Bites festival to reach "new consumers beyond the typical wine drinker," while Uruguay formally declared wine a "living culture" through cross-government policy integrating cultural heritage, industry strategy, and international diplomacy.

**Why it is a signal:** Wine is experiencing an identity crisis with younger consumers. The response is twofold: embedding in contemporary cultural contexts (festivals, music) and seeking institutional validation as heritage. Both strategies attempt to transcend the "just a beverage" positioning.

**Societal layer:** Status / Belonging

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Non-alcoholic brands can leapfrog wine's cultural positioning challenges by being native to contemporary experience design. Partner with cultural institutions, festivals, and experience platforms from inception rather than retrofitting.

**What to monitor:** Wine brand experiential marketing spend; cultural institution partnerships in beverage space; government recognition of beverage categories as cultural heritage.

---

### SIGNAL 5: Regulatory Pressure as Category Accelerant

**What happened:** European health experts called for "much higher taxes" on alcohol and unhealthy food to cover the costs they place on health services, citing 284,000 annual deaths from liver disease.

**Why it is a signal:** The externalised costs of alcohol consumption are entering mainstream policy discourse. This creates structural headwinds for alcohol (price increases, marketing restrictions) while positioning non-alcoholic alternatives as socially responsible choices deserving policy support.

**Societal layer:** Control

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Prepare for regulatory tailwinds. Non-alcoholic brands should engage with health policy conversations, position as part of public health solutions, and anticipate potential preferential tax treatment or marketing freedoms.

**What to monitor:** European alcohol taxation policy developments; WHO positioning on non-alcoholic alternatives; health ministry statements on beverage categories.

### SIGNAL 6: The Bifurcated Consumer

**What happened:** Modelo simultaneously launched an 8% ABV "bolder, higher-proof" variant AND a non-alcoholic Limón y Sal version, targeting the same Gen Z/Millennial demographic with both products.

**Why it is a signal:** The same consumers want both extremes—sometimes seeking intensity, sometimes seeking moderation. This isn't about different consumer segments but about the same individuals in different modes. Occasion-based identity is replacing fixed consumption patterns.

**Societal layer:** Identity / Pleasure

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Portfolio thinking becomes essential. Brands need presence across the intensity spectrum. Hospitality venues should offer clear pathways between alcoholic and non-alcoholic options for the same consumer across different occasions or even within the same evening.

**What to monitor:** Consumer research on occasion-switching behaviour; brand portfolio strategies spanning alcohol/non-alcohol; menu design innovations accommodating fluid consumption patterns.

---

### SIGNAL 7: Radlers as Youth Gateway

**What happened:** Asahi UK identified radlers (fruit-flavoured, often lower-alcohol beers) as having "significant growth potential" specifically because under-35s are "the most likely age group to be moderating yet the least likely to be buying into alcohol-free beer."

**Why it is a signal:** There's a gap between moderation intention and non-alcoholic adoption among young consumers. Radlers represent a middle path—flavour-forward, lower-intensity, socially acceptable. This suggests non-alcoholic beverages may need flavour and format innovation to capture moderating youth.

**Societal layer:** Pleasure / Belonging

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Non-alcoholic brands should study radler success factors: fruit-forward flavour profiles, summer/outdoor positioning, approachable formats. Consider whether "alcohol-free" positioning creates barriers that "low-alcohol" or "light" positioning avoids.

**What to monitor:** Radler category growth rates vs. non-alcoholic beer; flavour innovation in non-alcoholic space; consumer research on "alcohol-free" terminology perception.

---

### SIGNAL 8: Prosecco's Resilient Premiumisation

**What happened:** Despite flat overall sparkling wine markets, Prosecco grew 2% with Mionetto (world's best-selling Prosecco) growing 3%. Henkell Freixenet identified Prosecco, Crémant, and aperitivo as growth drivers alongside non-alcoholic.

**Why it is a signal:** Accessible luxury continues to outperform. Prosecco occupies a sweet spot: premium enough for status signalling, affordable enough for regular consumption, versatile enough for multiple occasions. This "democratic premium" positioning offers a template for non-alcoholic innovation.

**Societal layer:** Status / Pleasure

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Non-alcoholic sparkling wines and aperitivo-style drinks should study Prosecco's positioning: celebratory but casual, premium but accessible, Italian but universal. The aperitivo occasion specifically represents a high-potential non-alcoholic territory.

**What to monitor:** Non-alcoholic sparkling wine innovation; aperitivo-style non-alcoholic launches; Italian beverage brand extensions into non-alcoholic.

---

### SIGNAL 9: Club Culture as Creative Community

**What happened:** Dazed Club's Selfridges takeover celebrated "a new wave of club culture—one rooted as much in creativity and community as it is in nightlife," featuring life drawing sessions and archive talks alongside after-hours programming.

**Why it is a signal:** Nightlife is being redefined beyond alcohol-centric socialising. The creative, community, and cultural dimensions of club culture are being extracted and celebrated independently. This creates space for non-alcoholic participation in traditionally alcohol-dominated spaces.

**Societal layer:** Community / Belonging

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Partner with cultural institutions and creative communities redefining nightlife. Position non-alcoholic options as enabling extended creative engagement rather than limiting social participation. Sponsor daytime/early evening cultural programming.

**What to monitor:** Sober nightlife events; cultural institution evening programming; creative community gathering formats.

---

### SIGNAL 10: Korean Fashion's Heritage Ambition

**What happened:** Korean brand Amomento explicitly stated ambition to become "the world's first Korean heritage house," seeking to establish Korean fashion identity comparable to European counterparts.

**Why it is a signal:** Emerging cultural producers are no longer content with trend-driven positioning—they're building heritage narratives in real-time. This represents a broader pattern of non-Western cultural producers claiming legitimacy previously reserved for European traditions.

**Societal layer:** Identity / Status

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Non-alcoholic beverages can build heritage narratives from inception rather than waiting generations. Korean, Japanese, and other Asian beverage traditions (tea ceremonies, fermentation cultures) offer rich heritage foundations for premium non-alcoholic positioning.

**What to monitor:** Asian beverage brand premium positioning; heritage narrative construction in new categories; consumer response to non-European luxury beverage positioning.

## TOP 3 EXPERIMENTS

### Experiment 1: The Preparation Ritual Kit

**Hypothesis:** Gen Z and Millennial consumers will pay premium prices for non-alcoholic beverages positioned explicitly for "getting ready" occasions, packaged with ritual elements (glassware, playlist cards, preparation instructions) that recreate pre-digital social anticipation.

**Method:** Develop a limited-edition "Pre-Game" kit featuring a sophisticated non-alcoholic aperitivo, two branded glasses, a QR-linked playlist, and a ritual card with preparation instructions. Distribute through DTC and select lifestyle retailers. Price at 3x standard bottle price. Target 25-35 urban consumers through Instagram and TikTok influencer seeding focused on "getting ready" content creators.

**Success metric:** 70% sell-through within 8 weeks; 40% of purchasers share unboxing/preparation content; 25% repeat purchase of standalone bottle within 90 days.

---

### Experiment 2: The Functional Stack Menu

**Hypothesis:** Hospitality venues that explicitly communicate multiple functional benefits (alcohol-free + gut-friendly + anti-inflammatory) for non-alcoholic options will see increased non-alcoholic selection rates and higher average transaction values.

**Method:** Partner with 5 progressive restaurants/bars to redesign non-alcoholic menu sections with "functional benefit icons" (similar to allergen labelling) indicating: alcohol-free, gluten-free, gut-supportive, adaptogenic, low-sugar. Train staff on benefit communication. A/B test against control venues with standard menu descriptions over 12 weeks.

**Success metric:** 15% increase in non-alcoholic beverage selection rate; 20% increase in non-alcoholic beverage revenue per cover; measurable increase in staff confidence discussing non-alcoholic options.

---

### Experiment 3: The Cultural Institution Evening Series

**Hypothesis:** Non-alcoholic beverage brands that sponsor creative/cultural programming in traditionally alcohol-associated evening time slots will build stronger brand affinity and purchase intent than those using conventional marketing channels.

**Method:** Partner with a contemporary art gallery or cultural institution to sponsor a 6-week evening programme (7-10pm) featuring talks, workshops, and performances. Provide branded non-alcoholic beverages as the exclusive drink offering. Conduct pre/post brand awareness and purchase intent surveys among attendees. Compare cost-per-acquisition against digital advertising benchmarks.

**Success metric:** 30% increase in unaided brand awareness among attendees; 25% of attendees purchase product within 30 days; cost-per-acquisition below £15.
