## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Today's cultural landscape reveals a hospitality industry in active recalibration—balancing premiumisation pressures against accessibility demands, while navigating geopolitical trade disruptions and evolving consumer identity markers. The tension between craft authenticity and corporate ownership continues to simmer (Beavertown's new Heineken-appointed leadership), while economic realities force both producers and consumers to reassess value equations (Termini Bros actively lowering prices, Naked Wines pursuing profitable contraction).

A significant geographic pivot is underway: American spirits producers are abandoning traditional Western export markets for Southeast Asian stability, while sparkling wine giants target the region's "experimental, occasion-driven" young consumers. This isn't merely market diversification—it's a recognition that emerging middle classes in Vietnam, Singapore, and beyond represent the next generation of beverage culture formation.

The premiumisation narrative faces counter-currents. While Cava positions its Guarda Superior tier for fine wine credibility and Provence rosé producers emphasise terroir complexity over "easy drinking" stereotypes, a parallel movement demands accessible indulgence. The Philadelphia bakery lowering prices to preserve "neighbourhood business" identity signals consumer fatigue with luxury creep—a sentiment transferable to beverage experiences.

Non-alcoholic options continue their quiet institutionalisation: Asahi's Lord's Cricket Ground partnership prominently features Peroni Nastro Azzurro 0.0% alongside alcoholic offerings, normalising premium NA choices in prestigious social settings. This mainstreaming occurs without fanfare—the most powerful cultural shifts often do.

The AI revolution reaches hospitality's edges (Harvard's emergency triage study), foreshadowing service transformation. Meanwhile, social enterprises like 130 Primrose demonstrate that hospitality can serve as infrastructure for community repair, not merely consumption. The industry's purpose is expanding beyond pleasure delivery toward social utility.

## TREND SIGNALS

### SIGNAL 1: The Quiet Institutionalisation of Premium NA

**What happened:** Asahi's multi-year partnership with Lord's Cricket Ground explicitly features Peroni Nastro Azzurro 0.0% as part of its "expanded depth and breadth of choice," positioning non-alcoholic beer alongside Fuller's London Pride and craft offerings at one of Britain's most prestigious sporting venues.

**Why it is a signal:** Premium NA is no longer positioned as alternative or compromise—it's presented as equivalent choice within curated portfolios at elite venues. The language of "accommodating all tastes" frames moderation as preference, not restriction.

**Societal layer:** Status / belonging

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** NA brands must pursue prestige venue partnerships aggressively. The battle for normalisation is won through institutional presence, not marketing claims. Cricket grounds, racing venues, and members' clubs confer legitimacy that advertising cannot purchase.

**What to monitor:** Partnership announcements at Ascot, Wimbledon, Premier League stadiums; menu positioning (is NA listed alongside or separate from alcoholic options); pricing parity between NA and alcoholic equivalents.

### SIGNAL 2: The Southeast Asian Springboard Strategy

**What happened:** Both American whiskey producers (via DISCUS delegation to Singapore) and Henkell Freixenet are explicitly targeting Southeast Asia as priority growth markets, citing "elevated hospitality culture," tariff-free trade agreements, and young consumers who are "experimental, occasion-driven and lifestyle-orientated."

**Why it is a signal:** This represents a generational shift in where beverage culture is being formed. Western producers are no longer simply exporting existing products—they're positioning for influence over how emerging middle classes develop drinking rituals and preferences.

**Societal layer:** Identity / status

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Southeast Asia's young, health-conscious urban consumers represent an untapped NA market. Brands entering now can shape category expectations rather than competing against entrenched habits. The "Spritz" concept Henkell Freixenet is introducing suggests format innovation opportunities.

**What to monitor:** NA brand launches in Singapore, Vietnam, Thailand; local influencer partnerships; emergence of "sober curious" communities in Southeast Asian cities; hotel and airline lounge NA offerings in the region.

### SIGNAL 3: The Accessibility Counter-Movement

**What happened:** Philadelphia's century-old Termini Bros bakery actively lowered prices on family-oriented products (birthday cakes from $40 to $30, cupcakes from $5 to $3), explicitly rejecting the luxury pastry trajectory to preserve its identity as a "neighbourhood business."

**Why it is a signal:** This represents conscious resistance to premiumisation—a business choosing community embeddedness over margin maximisation. The framing of "family" as "Italian, meaning your neighbours, your co-workers" articulates a relational rather than transactional business model.

**Societal layer:** Community / belonging

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** There's emerging space for NA brands positioned on accessibility and everyday ritual rather than premium occasion. The "neighbourhood" positioning could translate to local taprooms, corner-store presence, and pricing that enables habitual rather than special-occasion consumption.

**What to monitor:** Consumer sentiment around "affordable indulgence"; success of value-positioned NA launches; community-focused hospitality concepts; backlash against $15+ mocktails.

### SIGNAL 4: The Craft Authenticity Tension

**What happened:** Heineken appointed an internal executive as Beavertown's new managing director, with corporate language about "continued growth" and "customer partnerships." This follows the brewery's full acquisition in 2022, after founder Logan Plant had been "very vocal on the importance of independently-owned 'craft' breweries being distinct from corporate macro beer companies."

**Why it is a signal:** The craft-to-corporate pipeline continues, but the tension remains unresolved. Heineken's insistence that Beavertown "continues to operate as a separate company with its own distinctive identity" acknowledges that authenticity matters—even if the claim strains credibility.

**Societal layer:** Identity / rebellion

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** NA brands have an authenticity advantage if they can maintain genuine independence. The "craft NA" positioning requires demonstrable separation from corporate beverage giants. Founder stories, transparent ownership structures, and community engagement become competitive moats.

**What to monitor:** Consumer response to Beavertown under new leadership; emergence of "indie NA" certification or community standards; success of founder-led NA brands versus corporate NA line extensions.

### SIGNAL 5: Terroir as Differentiation Strategy

**What happened:** Both Provence rosé producers and Cava's Guarda Superior category are actively repositioning away from "easy drinking" associations toward terroir complexity, structure, and gastronomy. Producers explicitly challenge perceptions that their wines lack place-based character.

**Why it is a signal:** Categories fighting commoditisation are reaching for wine's traditional status markers—terroir, ageing, food pairing—rather than competing on price or convenience. This premiumisation-through-education strategy requires consumer investment in learning.

**Societal layer:** Status / identity

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Premium NA can borrow this playbook: botanical sourcing stories, production method transparency, regional identity, and food pairing guidance. The "terroir of NA" might emphasise water sources, botanical origins, or fermentation cultures.

**What to monitor:** Consumer engagement with NA origin stories; success of NA "education" content; emergence of NA sommeliers or specialists; restaurant NA pairing menus.

### SIGNAL 6: Hospitality as Social Infrastructure

**What happened:** Chef Monica Galetti's 130 Primrose restaurant in London is designed specifically to recruit, train, and employ people affected by homelessness, offering paid work, accredited training, and career progression. The model includes charity partnerships and six-month employment contracts.

**Why it is a signal:** Hospitality venues are being reimagined as platforms for social repair, not merely consumption spaces. This extends the industry's purpose beyond pleasure delivery toward community utility—a significant expansion of what restaurants and bars can mean.

**Societal layer:** Community / belonging

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** NA brands can align with social-purpose hospitality, positioning moderation as part of a broader ethical consumption framework. Partnerships with social enterprises, employment programmes, and community spaces create differentiation beyond product attributes.

**What to monitor:** Growth of social enterprise hospitality; consumer willingness to pay premiums for "purpose-driven" venues; NA brand partnerships with community organisations; B-Corp or equivalent certifications in beverage sector.

### SIGNAL 7: The White Wine Awakening in Emerging Markets

**What happened:** Vietnam's wine market, though tiny (0.34% of alcohol consumption), is seeing white wine claim 8.6% greater share of total wine consumption year-over-year. Industry observers note evolution from "spirit-like" heavy reds toward lighter, fresher styles among younger urban consumers.

**Why it is a signal:** Emerging market palate development is skipping stages—moving directly toward lighter, more refreshing styles rather than replicating Western progression from heavy to light. This suggests openness to NA alternatives that deliver freshness and sophistication.

**Societal layer:** Identity / status

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** NA brands targeting emerging markets should emphasise freshness, elegance, and sophistication rather than mimicking heavy alcoholic styles. The "elevated hospitality culture" these consumers seek may be more receptive to NA than mature markets.

**What to monitor:** White wine and NA growth correlation in emerging markets; premium NA launches in Vietnam, Thailand, Indonesia; hospitality training programmes incorporating NA service.

### SIGNAL 8: The Profitable Contraction Model

**What happened:** Naked Wines reported "strong progress" with revenue around £200 million and improved profitability, following deliberate strategy to become "smaller but more profitable." Net cash rose from £3m to £33.4m, with inventory at five-year lows.

**Why it is a signal:** Growth-at-all-costs is being replaced by sustainable-scale thinking. The explicit framing of "health not seen since before 2023" suggests that right-sizing can be positioned positively rather than as failure.

**Societal layer:** Control

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** NA brands should resist pressure to scale prematurely. Profitability and community depth may matter more than volume growth. The DTC model Naked Wines pioneered offers lessons for NA brands building direct consumer relationships.

**What to monitor:** Investor appetite for profitable-but-smaller beverage companies; DTC NA brand unit economics; consumer loyalty metrics versus acquisition costs.

### SIGNAL 9: Parliamentary Drinking as Cultural Flashpoint

**What happened:** YouGov polling found 76% of Britons consider it unacceptable for MPs to drink alcohol before late-night votes, with 52% saying "completely unacceptable." Women and Scottish/Welsh respondents are most critical.

**Why it is a signal:** Public tolerance for alcohol in professional contexts is declining. The expectation that serious work requires sobriety is strengthening, with generational and gender dimensions suggesting this will intensify.

**Societal layer:** Control / status

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Professional and corporate hospitality represents significant NA opportunity. The "working dinner" and "business entertainment" occasions are ripe for NA normalisation as expectations around professional sobriety tighten.

**What to monitor:** Corporate entertainment policies; professional association guidance on alcohol at events; NA presence at conferences and business venues; insurance and liability considerations.

### SIGNAL 10: AI's Hospitality Adjacency

**What happened:** Harvard research found AI systems outperformed human doctors in emergency triage diagnoses, with researchers calling it a "profound change in technology that will reshape medicine."

**Why it is a signal:** While not directly hospitality-related, AI's demonstrated superiority in high-stakes human judgment tasks signals coming transformation across service industries. Hospitality's human touch may become its primary differentiation as AI handles operational complexity.

**Societal layer:** Control / identity

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** As AI transforms service efficiency, human connection becomes hospitality's irreplaceable value. NA experiences that emphasise craft, conversation, and community will differentiate against automated convenience. The bartender's role evolves from drink-maker to connection-facilitator.

**What to monitor:** AI adoption in hospitality operations; consumer response to automated versus human service; premium positioning of "human-crafted" experiences; NA brand storytelling emphasising human makers.

### SIGNAL 11: Protected Designation Enforcement Intensifies

**What happened:** The Prosecco Consorzio is pursuing UK High Court action against a London drinks business over alleged misuse of the Prosecco name, testing post-Brexit geographical indication protections.

**Why it is a signal:** Category protection is being actively enforced, establishing precedents that will shape how beverage names can be used. This has implications for NA products using established category terminology.

**Societal layer:** Identity / status

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** NA brands must navigate naming carefully—"non-alcoholic prosecco" or similar constructions may face legal challenge. Original naming and category creation may be safer than borrowing established wine/spirit terminology.

**What to monitor:** Outcomes of geographical indication cases; regulatory guidance on NA naming conventions; emergence of protected NA designations; consumer understanding of NA category terminology.

## TOP 3 EXPERIMENTS

### Experiment 1: The Prestige Venue NA Integration Audit

**Hypothesis:** Premium NA beverages gain legitimacy and trial when positioned alongside alcoholic equivalents at prestigious venues, rather than in separate "alternatives" sections. Menu integration drives normalisation more effectively than dedicated NA promotion.

**Method:** Partner with three prestige venues (members' club, sporting venue, cultural institution) to test two menu formats over 90 days: (A) integrated listing where NA options appear within relevant categories (e.g., NA beer with beers, NA aperitif with aperitifs) at price parity; (B) traditional separate "non-alcoholic" section. Track order rates, repeat orders, and customer feedback on perceived quality and social comfort.

**Success metric:** 25% higher NA order rate in integrated format; qualitative feedback indicating reduced stigma or "special request" perception; venue willingness to adopt integrated format permanently.

---

### Experiment 2: The Southeast Asian Format Innovation Lab

**Hypothesis:** Young Southeast Asian consumers, being "experimental and occasion-driven" without entrenched drinking rituals, will adopt novel NA formats (ready-to-drink botanical spritzes, functional aperitifs, ceremonial tea-cocktails) more readily than Western consumers attached to existing categories.

**Method:** Develop three novel NA formats specifically for Singapore market launch, working with local hospitality partners to create serving rituals and occasions. Formats should emphasise visual appeal (Instagram-readiness), functional benefits (energy, relaxation, digestion), and social sharing. Test through pop-up activations at lifestyle venues over 60 days, measuring trial, repeat purchase intent, and social sharing.

**Success metric:** 40% trial-to-repeat-intent conversion; organic social sharing rate exceeding 15% of purchasers; identification of one format with clear product-market fit for broader rollout.

---

### Experiment 3: The Neighbourhood NA Taproom Model

**Hypothesis:** An NA taproom positioned explicitly on accessibility and everyday ritual (not premium occasion) can build sustainable community loyalty and challenge the assumption that NA must compete on premiumisation.

**Method:** Launch a 90-day pop-up NA taproom in a neighbourhood setting (not city centre), with pricing positioned below typical bar prices, emphasis on "regulars" culture, and programming focused on community gathering (book clubs, craft nights, neighbourhood meetings). Track visit frequency, community group adoption, and customer acquisition cost versus lifetime value.

**Success metric:** 30% of customers visiting more than twice monthly; three community groups adopting venue as regular meeting space; customer acquisition cost below £5 with projected 12-month LTV exceeding £100.
