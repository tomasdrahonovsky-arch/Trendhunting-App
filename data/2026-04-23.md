## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Today's cultural landscape reveals a fundamental tension between authenticity and performance—across relationships, consumption, and identity. The drinks industry is navigating this through strategic repositioning: Treasury Wine Estates restructures around regional accountability while Heineken 0.0 recruits Serena Williams to legitimise non-alcoholic beer through elite athletic performance. Meanwhile, auction houses report collectors increasingly seeking younger vintages and organic wines, signalling a shift from passive accumulation toward active, values-aligned consumption.

The hospitality sector faces geopolitical disruption (Gulf Bar Show postponed due to Iran conflict) while simultaneously experiencing a steakhouse renaissance that prioritises theatrical experience over mere sustenance. This paradox—retreat from uncertainty coupled with appetite for bold, dramatic spaces—reflects a broader cultural need for controlled intensity.

In relationships and identity, the signals are stark: Vice's coverage of "swipe baiting" and singlehood psychology reveals exhaustion with performative dating, while Dazed's cyberdeck DIY movement represents active rebellion against tech oligarchy. The body itself becomes contested territory—from Jewel Yang's sacred makeup photography to record cancer diagnoses straining healthcare systems.

For non-alcoholic beverage strategists, the critical insight is this: consumers are increasingly sophisticated at detecting inauthenticity. Williams' Heineken 0.0 partnership works because padel represents genuine lifestyle integration, not borrowed credibility. The wine auction shift toward younger vintages and organics mirrors broader demand for transparency and immediacy over mystique and age. Brands that can demonstrate genuine alignment with consumer values—environmental stewardship (Yellowstone Bourbon's conservation work), regional authenticity (Chinese domestic wineries gaining professional distribution), or functional benefit (AI visibility tools for wineries)—will outperform those relying on legacy positioning alone.

The cultural moment demands proof, not promise.

## TREND SIGNALS

### SIGNAL 1: Athletic Legitimisation of Non-Alcoholic Beer

**What happened:** Serena Williams signed a multi-year partnership with Heineken 0.0, tied specifically to padel—a sport experiencing explosive growth among affluent, health-conscious demographics. The activation involved Williams surprising players at a Miami padel club, emphasising spontaneous connection over staged endorsement.

**Why it is a signal:** This represents the maturation of non-alcoholic beer from "compromise choice" to "performance enabler." Williams explicitly frames Heineken 0.0 as supporting her ability to "stay active and keep moving"—positioning zero-alcohol as additive rather than subtractive.

**Societal layer:** Status / Identity

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Non-alcoholic brands should pursue partnerships with emerging sports (padel, pickleball, climbing) rather than established ones. The opportunity lies in becoming synonymous with new athletic communities before competitors establish presence.

**What to monitor:** Padel club membership demographics; social media sentiment around "sober athletes"; Playtomic booking data for alcohol-free venue events.

### SIGNAL 2: Conservation as Brand Equity

**What happened:** Yellowstone Bourbon's donation to the National Parks Conservation Association crossed $1 million since 2018, with the partnership now integrated into the brand's 250th Declaration of Independence anniversary campaign.

**Why it is a signal:** Long-term environmental partnerships are transitioning from CSR checkbox to core brand narrative. The seven-generation distilling heritage combined with conservation stewardship creates an authenticity moat competitors cannot easily replicate.

**Societal layer:** Belonging / Spirituality

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Non-alcoholic brands should identify conservation or community causes with genuine connection to their origin story or production process. Multi-year commitments with measurable milestones outperform one-off donations.

**What to monitor:** Consumer research on "brand purpose fatigue"; competitor conservation partnerships; National Park visitation demographics.

### SIGNAL 3: Auction Behaviour Signals Consumption Philosophy Shift

**What happened:** iDealwine's annual barometer revealed 55% of wines sold in 2025 were over 20 years old, down from 69% in 2024. Organic wines now account for 30% of volume and 36% of value. Collectors are increasingly acquiring recent vintages at auction.

**Why it is a signal:** The traditional wine investment thesis—buy young, cellar, sell mature—is fragmenting. Collectors now value immediate drinkability and production ethics alongside investment potential. This reflects broader cultural impatience with deferred gratification.

**Societal layer:** Control / Status

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Premium non-alcoholic beverages should emphasise "drink now" positioning with transparent production narratives. Consider limited releases with clear organic/sustainable credentials designed for immediate consumption rather than collection.

**What to monitor:** Secondary market activity for premium non-alcoholic spirits; auction house interest in functional beverages; collector community forums.

**Why it is a signal:** In uncertain times, consumers seek spaces offering theatrical intensity within controlled parameters. The steakhouse provides predictable luxury—you know what you're getting—while new iterations add surprise elements that reward repeat visits.

**Societal layer:** Pleasure / Status

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Non-alcoholic beverage programmes in upscale dining should match the theatrical confidence of the food experience. Bold presentations, tableside preparations, and sommelier-level service for zero-proof options will meet elevated expectations.

**What to monitor:** Non-alcoholic pairing menu adoption at high-end steakhouses; consumer reviews mentioning beverage programmes; chef-driven non-alcoholic collaborations.

## TOP 3 EXPERIMENTS

### Experiment 1: Padel Club Non-Alcoholic Tap Takeover

**Hypothesis:** Emerging sport venues (padel, pickleball clubs) represent underserved distribution channels where non-alcoholic beverages can become default choice rather than alternative, building brand association with athletic identity.

**Method:** Partner with 5-10 padel clubs in target markets for exclusive non-alcoholic tap presence during a 90-day pilot. Provide branded glassware, staff training on product story, and post-match sampling programmes. Track per-player consumption, repeat purchase behaviour, and social media mentions.

**Success metric:** 40% of players ordering non-alcoholic option post-match; 25% brand mention rate in player social posts; club operators requesting programme extension.

---

### Experiment 2: "Open Source" Recipe Transparency Campaign

**Hypothesis:** Consumers exhibiting DIY/maker mentality will develop stronger brand loyalty toward non-alcoholic beverages that share production transparency, including recipes and sourcing details—treating openness as trust signal rather than competitive vulnerability.

**Method:** Publish complete ingredient list, sourcing origins, and simplified "home version" recipe for one flagship product. Create content series documenting production process. Monitor competitor response, consumer engagement, and whether transparency drives trial or cannibalises sales.

**Success metric:** 15% increase in first-time purchasers citing "transparency" as motivation; home-recipe content generating 100K+ organic impressions; no measurable sales cannibalisation from DIY attempts.

---

### Experiment 3: AI Visibility Audit and Narrative Seeding

**Hypothesis:** Non-alcoholic beverage brands that proactively manage their presence in AI-generated recommendations will capture disproportionate discovery traffic as consumers shift from traditional search to conversational AI queries.

**Method:** Conduct baseline audit of how major AI systems (ChatGPT, Perplexity, Claude, Gemini) describe the brand and category. Identify narrative gaps and competitor positioning. Develop content strategy specifically designed for AI training data inclusion (structured data, authoritative sourcing, clear brand positioning). Re-audit at 60 and 120 days.

**Success metric:** Brand mentioned in 50% of relevant AI-generated recommendations (up from baseline); measurable traffic from AI-referred discovery; category leadership positioning in AI responses.
