## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Today's cultural landscape reveals a drinks industry in active recalibration—navigating between heritage preservation and contemporary relevance while consumers increasingly demand authenticity, sustainability, and experiences that transcend the liquid itself. The tension between tradition and transformation is palpable across multiple fronts.

The most significant pattern emerging is the strategic repositioning of "unfashionable" categories. Just as Guinness has successfully captured younger drinkers through cultural partnerships (the JW Anderson collaboration now expanding to 17 pieces), traditional British bitter and cider are being reframed for new audiences. Cider's 19% increase in serves per occasion—now rivalling beer—demonstrates that perceived "legacy" categories can achieve relevance when positioned for contemporary consumption contexts, particularly food pairing.

Simultaneously, we're witnessing the institutionalisation of drinks brands as cultural patrons. Sunrise Beverages' £25,000 investment in the ICA represents a broader shift where beverage companies seek legitimacy through arts and culture rather than traditional advertising. This mirrors the Hope & Sesame bar's pivot to partnering with Lee Kum Kee condiments as spirit brand support diminishes—a creative response to changing commercial realities.

The sustainability premium remains robust despite economic pressures. PwC data showing consumers will pay 9.7% more for sustainable products, even as 31% cite inflation as their primary concern, signals that environmental values have become non-negotiable rather than aspirational.

For non-alcoholic beverage strategists, the message is clear: category heritage is an asset, not a liability, when paired with cultural credibility and contemporary positioning. The opportunity lies in occupying the spaces where tradition meets transformation—whether through arts partnerships, fashion collaborations, or reimagined consumption occasions.

## TREND SIGNALS

### SIGNAL 1: Heritage Brands as Cultural Patrons

**What happened:** Sunrise Beverages invested £25,000 in London's Institute of Contemporary Arts for a two-year partnership, including bar refurbishment and prominent brand placement across ordering systems and menus.

**Why it is a signal:** Beverage companies are shifting from transactional sponsorship to institutional patronage, seeking cultural legitimacy and community embeddedness rather than mere visibility. This represents a maturation of brand-building strategy.

**Societal layer:** Status / belonging

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** NA brands should pursue partnerships with cultural institutions, independent cinemas, or community spaces where their presence signals taste and values alignment rather than commercial intrusion. The ICA audience—culturally engaged, discerning—maps directly onto premium NA consumer profiles.

**What to monitor:** Arts institution partnership announcements; beverage brand presence at gallery openings, theatre programmes, and cultural festivals; consumer sentiment around brand-culture alignment.

---

### SIGNAL 2: The Rehabilitation of "Unfashionable" Categories

**What happened:** Industry voices are questioning whether traditional British bitter could follow Guinness's trajectory in winning younger drinkers, despite cask ale volumes falling 7.1% year-on-year to 1.28 million hectolitres.

**Why it is a signal:** The Guinness playbook—cultural partnerships, fashion collaborations, social media presence—has created a template for repositioning heritage categories. The question is no longer whether "old" drinks can be relevant, but how to execute the transformation.

**Societal layer:** Identity / rebellion

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** NA brands with heritage ingredients (botanicals, traditional brewing methods, regional provenance) should lean into authenticity rather than chasing trend-driven innovation. The "anti-fashion" positioning may resonate with consumers fatigued by constant novelty.

**What to monitor:** Social media sentiment around "dad drinks" and heritage categories; craft bitter taproom openings; bartender interest in classic styles.

---

### SIGNAL 3: Cider's Contextual Expansion

**What happened:** Cider now represents over 9% of on-trade wet sales, with serves per occasion up 19% to 3.2—nearly matching beer at 3.3. Crucially, 40% of cider occasions now occur while eating.

**Why it is a signal:** Cider has successfully repositioned from a standalone drink to a food-pairing beverage, expanding its consumption occasions. This contextual flexibility is driving growth despite overall volume pressures.

**Societal layer:** Pleasure / belonging

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** NA beverages must aggressively pursue food-pairing positioning. The 40% food-occasion figure for cider suggests significant untapped potential for NA drinks designed specifically for meal accompaniment—not as wine substitutes, but as distinct pairing options.

**What to monitor:** Menu integration of NA pairings; chef and sommelier attitudes toward NA food matching; restaurant NA beverage programme development.

### SIGNAL 4: Fashion-Beverage Convergence Deepens

**What happened:** Guinness and JW Anderson expanded their collaboration from 4 to 17 pieces, featuring Joe Alwyn and Little Simz, with designs inspired by pub interiors, brewery uniforms, and beer mats transformed into wearable items.

**Why it is a signal:** This isn't licensing—it's cultural co-creation. The expansion from a capsule to a full collection indicates commercial success and consumer appetite for beverage brands as lifestyle signifiers beyond consumption.

**Societal layer:** Identity / status

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Premium NA brands should explore fashion and lifestyle collaborations that extend brand meaning beyond the bottle. The pub aesthetic—warmth, community, texture—translates into merchandise and experiences that reinforce brand identity.

**What to monitor:** Fashion week beverage brand presence; designer interest in drinks iconography; consumer willingness to wear beverage-branded items as status markers.

### SIGNAL 5: Cocktail Bars Pivot to Non-Spirit Partnerships

**What happened:** Hope & Sesame, ranked #7 on Asia's 50 Best Bars, partnered with 138-year-old condiment brand Lee Kum Kee for a cocktail menu, explicitly citing dried-up spirit brand support as motivation.

**Why it is a signal:** The economics of bar partnerships are shifting. As spirit brands reduce support, bars are finding creative alternatives—and discovering that food brands offer both funding and genuine flavour innovation opportunities.

**Societal layer:** Pleasure / community

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** NA brands have an opening to fill the partnership gap left by retreating spirit companies. Bars seeking alternative revenue streams may be more receptive to NA brand collaborations than ever before.

**What to monitor:** Bar partnership announcements with non-traditional brands; bartender experimentation with food ingredients; Asia's 50 Best Bars trends.

### SIGNAL 6: Sustainability Premium Holds Despite Inflation

**What happened:** PwC's survey of 20,000+ consumers found 80% willing to pay more for sustainable products, with an average premium tolerance of 9.7%—even as 31% cite inflation as their biggest consumption risk.

**Why it is a signal:** Sustainability has transitioned from aspirational to expected. The premium tolerance during economic pressure indicates values-based purchasing is now embedded rather than discretionary.

**Societal layer:** Identity / control

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Sustainability claims must be substantive and verifiable. Consumers are paying attention to production methods (40%), eco-packaging (38%), and nature/water impact (34%). Greenwashing risk is elevated as scrutiny intensifies.

**What to monitor:** Consumer response to sustainability certifications; social media callouts of greenwashing; B Corp and similar certification adoption rates.

### SIGNAL 8: Prosecco Producers Embrace Mixology—Conditionally

**What happened:** Leading Prosecco producers expressed support for cocktail use, but with a consistent caveat: the wine must remain the "hero ingredient" with its identity respected rather than masked.

**Why it is a signal:** Premium producers are negotiating the tension between volume growth (cocktails) and brand integrity (quality perception). The conditional acceptance suggests a framework for how heritage products can participate in mixology culture.

**Societal layer:** Status / pleasure

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** NA brands entering cocktail/mocktail spaces should establish clear guidelines for how their products should be used—positioning themselves as premium ingredients worthy of respect rather than generic mixers.

**What to monitor:** Bartender treatment of premium NA ingredients; cocktail menu language around NA components; producer-bar collaboration models.

### SIGNAL 9: Climate Anxiety Manifests in Consumption Choices

**What happened:** PwC found 85% of consumers experiencing climate change effects in daily life, with 46% buying more sustainable products and 32% changing their diets as a result.

**Why it is a signal:** Climate concern has moved from abstract worry to behavioural change. The 32% dietary shift figure is particularly significant for beverage brands, as food and drink choices become expressions of environmental values.

**Societal layer:** Control / identity

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** NA brands should communicate climate credentials clearly and specifically. Vague sustainability claims will be insufficient; consumers want to understand water usage, carbon footprint, and supply chain impacts.

**What to monitor:** Consumer questions about environmental impact; social media discussion of "climate-friendly" consumption; regulatory developments around environmental claims.

### SIGNAL 10: Regional Pride as Competitive Advantage

**What happened:** Champagne Drappier is making significant investments in the Aube region, with managing director Michel Drappier noting that being from a "remote place" has shifted from embarrassment to pride over 50 years.

**Why it is a signal:** Terroir and regional identity are becoming competitive differentiators rather than limitations. The "underdog region" narrative resonates with consumers seeking authenticity beyond established prestige markers.

**Societal layer:** Belonging / identity

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** NA brands should consider regional sourcing stories as brand assets. Ingredients from lesser-known regions can signal discovery and authenticity rather than second-tier status.

**What to monitor:** Consumer interest in ingredient provenance; regional food and drink tourism trends; "undiscovered" region marketing success.

### SIGNAL 11: Insect Decline as Ecosystem Warning

**What happened:** Research found 88.5% of North American insects and arachnids have unknown conservation status, with 94.7% of at-risk species receiving no legal protection.

**Why it is a signal:** Biodiversity collapse threatens agricultural systems, including ingredients essential to beverage production. The knowledge gap about insect populations represents systemic risk to supply chains dependent on pollination and ecosystem health.

**Societal layer:** Control / spirituality

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Brands reliant on botanical ingredients should assess supply chain vulnerability to pollinator decline. Proactive investment in biodiversity protection could become both risk mitigation and marketing differentiation.

**What to monitor:** Pollinator population data; agricultural supply disruptions; regenerative farming adoption in beverage ingredient sourcing.

## TOP 3 EXPERIMENTS

### Experiment 1: Cultural Institution NA Residency Programme

**Hypothesis:** Partnering with arts institutions for exclusive NA beverage service will elevate brand perception among culturally engaged consumers and create earned media opportunities that traditional advertising cannot achieve.

**Method:** Approach three mid-tier cultural venues (independent cinemas, galleries, performance spaces) with a proposal for 6-month exclusive NA beverage partnerships. Provide staff training, custom glassware, and menu integration. Track brand sentiment, social media mentions, and direct sales. Compare against control venues without partnership.

**Success metric:** 25% increase in brand awareness among target demographic (25-45, culturally engaged, urban); 15% sales lift in retail channels within 5-mile radius of partner venues; minimum 10 earned media placements.

---

### Experiment 2: Food-First NA Pairing Menu Development

**Hypothesis:** NA beverages positioned as food-pairing options (not alcohol substitutes) will achieve higher per-cover sales and repeat orders than those positioned as standalone drinks.

**Method:** Partner with 5 restaurants across cuisine types to develop dedicated NA pairing menus with chef collaboration. Create specific pairing recommendations for each dish. A/B test against standard NA beverage list presentation. Measure per-cover NA beverage revenue, customer feedback, and reorder rates over 3 months.

**Success metric:** 40% increase in NA beverage attachment rate to food orders; average 2.5 NA serves per dining occasion (matching cider benchmark); 70% positive customer feedback on pairing recommendations.

---

### Experiment 3: Bartender Partnership Programme for Spirit-Reduced Venues

**Hypothesis:** Bars experiencing reduced spirit brand support will be receptive to NA brand partnerships that offer both financial support and genuine creative collaboration opportunities.

**Method:** Identify 10 cocktail bars that have publicly noted reduced spirit brand support or expressed interest in NA innovation. Offer partnership packages including product supply, staff training, menu development support, and modest financial contribution. Track cocktail menu NA inclusion, bartender social media advocacy, and consumer trial rates over 6 months.

**Success metric:** 8 of 10 bars maintain partnership beyond initial term; minimum 3 signature NA cocktails developed per venue; 50% increase in NA category sales at partner venues; 5 bartender-generated social media posts per venue.
