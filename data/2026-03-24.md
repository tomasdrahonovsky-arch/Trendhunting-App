## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Today's cultural landscape reveals a fascinating tension between authenticity-seeking and technological mediation, playing out across beverage, hospitality, and lifestyle sectors. The dominant theme is **curated simplification meeting experiential depth**—consumers want complexity made accessible, whether that's Portuguese wine regions unified under one brand, cocktails translated into tangible design objects, or personalised Guinness pints that turn drinking into shareable content.

Three interconnected currents define this moment:

**1. The Democratisation of Expertise:** From Esporão's "Nós Somos" range simplifying Portugal's wine geography for UK shoppers to Damm's fridge packs making no/low beer more accessible, brands are recognising that consumers want quality without gatekeeping. The death of Michel Rolland—the original "flying winemaker"—marks the symbolic end of an era where expertise was concentrated in singular figures; the future belongs to distributed, accessible knowledge.

**2. Experience as Object:** Himkok's "Designed by Sipping" menu, which transforms cocktails into physical design pieces, and Guinness's viral "Stoutie" personalisation represent a broader shift toward making ephemeral experiences tangible and shareable. In an age of digital saturation, physical artifacts of experience carry new cultural currency.

**3. Daytime as the New Nighttime:** YSL Beauty's Block Party in Madrid (4pm-10pm) signals Gen Z's fundamental restructuring of social temporality. Going out no longer requires staying up late—a shift with profound implications for beverage occasions and hospitality design.

The underlying driver across all signals is a generation seeking **meaningful moderation**—not abstinence, but intentionality. They want experiences worth having, products worth understanding, and moments worth remembering, all within boundaries that serve their wellbeing rather than compromise it.

## TREND SIGNALS

### SIGNAL 1: The Unified Origin Story

**What happened:** Portuguese producer Esporão is launching "Nós Somos," a UK-specific range bringing wines from three distinct regions (Vinho Verde, Douro, Alentejo) under one brand at Morrisons, specifically designed to simplify Portugal for British shoppers unfamiliar with the country's wine geography.

**Why it is a signal:** This represents a fundamental shift from terroir-as-complexity to terroir-as-narrative. Rather than expecting consumers to become experts, brands are becoming translators—curating regional diversity into coherent, accessible stories. The collaboration with London consultancy Anew and Portuguese landscape painter Abel Mota shows how cultural authenticity can be preserved while removing barriers to entry.

**Societal layer:** Belonging / Status

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Non-alcoholic brands with complex ingredient stories (adaptogens, botanicals, regional sourcing) should consider unified brand architectures that tell coherent origin stories rather than expecting consumers to decode complexity. A single NA brand could span multiple functional benefits under one narrative umbrella.

**What to monitor:** Supermarket ranging decisions around "simplified premium" propositions; consumer research on regional literacy versus brand trust; success metrics of Nós Somos launch.

---

### SIGNAL 2: The Tangible Ephemeral

**What happened:** Oslo's Himkok bar launched "Designed by Sipping," a cocktail menu developed with Studio Sløyd where each of 13 drinks is paired with a one-off design object (glass, ceramics, textiles, wood) that translates the drink's flavour into physical form.

**Why it is a signal:** This inverts the traditional relationship between drink and vessel. The object isn't a container—it's a translation. In an experience economy saturated with the forgettable, this represents a new frontier: making the ephemeral permanent. The collaboration spans "music, fashion and photography," suggesting hospitality venues increasingly see themselves as cultural platforms rather than service providers.

**Societal layer:** Pleasure / Status

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** NA beverage brands could partner with designers to create limited-edition objects that embody their flavour profiles—not merchandise, but artistic interpretations. This creates collectibility, social currency, and a reason to return that transcends the liquid itself.

**What to monitor:** Secondary market for Himkok design objects; other bars adopting designer collaborations; consumer willingness to pay premium for "experience artifacts."

### SIGNAL 3: The Daylight Economy

**What happened:** YSL Beauty's Block Party in Madrid ran from 4pm to 10pm, with the article explicitly noting this reflects "the growing centrality of daytime parties to gen Z social life" and that "going out out no longer requires staying up past your [bedtime]."

**Why it is a signal:** This isn't a pandemic hangover—it's a permanent restructuring of social temporality. Gen Z is rejecting the assumption that meaningful social experiences require darkness, late hours, and alcohol-fuelled endurance. The daylight economy creates entirely new occasion spaces where non-alcoholic options aren't compromises but natural fits.

**Societal layer:** Community / Control

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** The 4pm-10pm window is prime territory for NA beverages. Brands should develop products and marketing specifically for "afternoon-to-evening" occasions—sophisticated enough for social settings, functional enough for the hours ahead. Hospitality venues should consider dedicated daytime programming with NA-forward menus.

**What to monitor:** Venue booking patterns by time of day; event start times trending earlier; emergence of "day party" as distinct category in hospitality.

### SIGNAL 4: The Personalised Pour

**What happened:** Guinness's "Stoutie Experience"—printing personalised selfies onto pint foam—has gone viral on social media, available at the Guinness Storehouse and Open Gate Brewery Covent Garden, with hints of expansion to "selected venues."

**Why it is a signal:** This transforms consumption into content creation and the product into a marketing vehicle. Each personalised pint becomes user-generated advertising. More significantly, it represents beverage brands embracing technology not for efficiency but for emotional resonance and shareability. The €6-8 premium suggests consumers will pay for personalisation that creates social currency.

**Societal layer:** Identity / Status

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** NA brands should explore personalisation technologies that create shareable moments. This could range from custom label printing to AI-generated flavour profiles based on personal data. The key insight: the experience of customisation may matter more than the customisation itself.

**What to monitor:** Adoption of beverage personalisation tech across categories; consumer willingness to pay for personalised experiences; social media engagement rates for personalised versus standard product posts.

### SIGNAL 5: The Fridge Pack Legitimisation

**What happened:** Damm is launching fridge pack formats of Estrella Damm 0.0% and Damm Lemon in UK retail, explicitly responding to consumers wanting "to recreate quality drinking experiences at home" and the growth of "flavoured lager and alcohol-free" categories.

**Why it is a signal:** The fridge pack format signals category maturation. When NA beer moves from single-bottle trial purchases to multi-pack pantry stocking, it indicates habitual consumption rather than occasional substitution. Damm's £70 million UK investment and ambition to become "the largest independent beer and total beverages company in the UK" shows major players treating NA as core strategy, not side project.

**Societal layer:** Control / Pleasure

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Format strategy matters as much as liquid development. NA brands should consider how packaging signals intended consumption occasion—multi-packs for home stocking, premium singles for on-premise trial, variety packs for exploration. The fridge pack says "this belongs in your regular rotation."

**What to monitor:** Multi-pack versus single-serve sales ratios for NA beverages; retailer shelf space allocation for NA multi-packs; repeat purchase rates by format.

### SIGNAL 6: The Value Resilience Paradox

**What happened:** JD Wetherspoon reported 4.8% like-for-like sales growth with drinks up 7.0%, even as adjusted profit fell 32% due to rising costs. Analysts note the chain's "long-standing reliance on high volumes and low prices faces increasing pressure."

**Why it is a signal:** Consumers are still going out, but the economics of value hospitality are breaking. This creates a bifurcation: either venues race to the bottom on price (unsustainable) or they must add value that justifies higher prices. The 10.9% increase in customer numbers suggests demand exists—the question is how to monetise it sustainably.

**Societal layer:** Community / Belonging

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Value-focused venues need margin-enhancing products. Premium NA beverages could offer higher margins than discounted alcohol while appealing to health-conscious consumers. Wetherspoon's challenge is an opportunity for NA brands positioned as "affordable premium."

**What to monitor:** Pub chain menu pricing strategies; NA beverage margin comparisons to alcoholic equivalents; consumer price sensitivity research by category.

### SIGNAL 7: The Champagne Confidence

**What happened:** Pol Roger's MD James Simpson MW expressed strong confidence in Champagne's UK future despite economic headwinds, noting the category is "remarkably resilient" and that English sparkling wine hasn't significantly cannibalised sales—"the two seem to be co-existing happily together."

**Why it is a signal:** This challenges the zero-sum assumption that premium categories must compete destructively. Champagne's resilience suggests consumers maintain category loyalty for specific occasions while exploring alternatives for others. The effectiveness of supermarket discounting (even loss-making £9.99 bottles) indicates price remains a powerful trial driver even for prestige products.

**Societal layer:** Status / Pleasure

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Premium NA sparkling wines should position as occasion-specific rather than universal substitutes. The Champagne insight suggests consumers can hold multiple products in their repertoire for different moments. NA brands should identify and own specific occasions rather than competing across all sparkling moments.

**What to monitor:** Occasion-based consumption research for sparkling categories; co-purchase data (Champagne + NA sparkling in same basket); premium NA sparkling trial rates following promotional pricing.

The fusion of digital culture (cybersigilism) with physical adornment suggests a generation comfortable blending online and offline identity markers. For beverages, this signals appetite for visual complexity and personalisation.

**Societal layer:** Identity / Rebellion

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** NA brands targeting younger consumers should consider maximalist visual identities that invite customisation and self-expression. Packaging could incorporate elements designed for personalisation—sticker sheets, customisable labels, or designs that encourage consumer modification and sharing.

**What to monitor:** Beauty trend crossover into beverage aesthetics; consumer-modified packaging shared on social media; maximalist versus minimalist design performance in A/B testing.

### SIGNAL 9: The Limerence Economy

**What happened:** Polly Barton's debut novel "What Am I, A Deer?" is described as unpacking "the highs and lows of limerence"—the state of intense romantic obsession—with a tone that is "confessional and clinical, dissecting and shameless."

**Why it is a signal:** Cultural products exploring emotional extremity with analytical distance suggest a generation seeking to understand their feelings rather than simply experience them. This "feeling about feelings" meta-awareness creates space for products that acknowledge emotional complexity rather than promising simple mood states.

**Societal layer:** Mental health / Identity

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Functional NA beverages often promise singular emotional outcomes (calm, focus, energy). There may be opportunity for products that acknowledge emotional complexity—drinks for "processing," for "sitting with uncertainty," for "feeling everything at once." Authenticity may lie in complexity, not simplicity.

**What to monitor:** Emotional vocabulary in beverage marketing; consumer response to nuanced versus simple benefit claims; literary/cultural products exploring emotional complexity.

### SIGNAL 10: The Appearance Anxiety Epidemic

**What happened:** A Guardian piece explores how "looksmaxxing" culture is reshaping young men's relationship with appearance, turning "a very private struggle—coming to terms with one's own appearance—into a site of competition and ridicule." Actor Barry Keoghan's admission that online abuse about his appearance sometimes makes him not want to go outside is cited.

**Why it is a signal:** Male appearance anxiety is becoming culturally visible in ways previously reserved for discussions of women's body image. This creates both vulnerability and opportunity—products that help men feel better about themselves without explicitly addressing insecurity may find receptive audiences. The social media amplification of appearance criticism accelerates this trend.

**Societal layer:** Identity / Mental health

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Functional beverages targeting skin health, "glow," or appearance-adjacent benefits may find growing male audiences, but messaging must be carefully calibrated to avoid triggering the very anxieties they address. Hospitality spaces that feel "safe" from appearance judgment may become increasingly valued.

**What to monitor:** Male-targeted beauty/wellness beverage launches; social media discourse around male appearance; venue design trends emphasising comfort over display.

The "discovery" narrative that drives interest in Chinese wine could apply to NA products from emerging markets with genuine provenance stories.

### SIGNAL 12: The Hyper-Regional American Food Wave

**What happened:** Manchester is experiencing a wave of "hyper-regional" American food, with Fat Pat's (serving "truncheon-sized sandwiches" from a hole-in-the-wall) achieving viral success through "word-of-mouth, social media virality and a studiedly underground brand identity."

**Why it is a signal:** Globalisation is becoming granular. It's no longer "American food" but specific regional American food—Philadelphia hoagies, not generic subs. This hyper-specificity creates authenticity claims that generic categories cannot match. The "studiedly underground" positioning shows how scarcity and difficulty (queuing in an alley) become features, not bugs.

**Societal layer:** Belonging / Status

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** NA beverages could adopt hyper-regional positioning—not "American craft soda" but "Brooklyn egg cream" or "New Orleans chicory coffee." Specificity creates story, story creates value, value justifies premium. The inconvenience of acquisition (limited availability, specific venues) may enhance rather than diminish appeal.

**What to monitor:** Hyper-regional food concepts expanding; consumer knowledge of regional American food traditions; scarcity as marketing strategy effectiveness.

## TOP 3 EXPERIMENTS

### Experiment 1: The Afternoon Social Menu

**Hypothesis:** A dedicated 3pm-8pm "Afternoon Social" NA beverage menu, positioned explicitly for the daylight economy, will outperform standard all-day NA offerings in both trial and repeat purchase among 21-35 year olds.

**Method:** Partner with 3-5 hospitality venues in a single city to develop and test a time-specific NA menu available only during afternoon-to-early-evening hours. The menu should feature 6-8 drinks with names, descriptions, and functional profiles designed for the "still have things to do today" occasion. Include light caffeine options, adaptogens for social ease, and sophisticated flavour profiles. Track sales velocity, customer demographics, time-of-day ordering patterns, and repeat visits. Run for 12 weeks with weekly data collection.

**Success metric:** 25% higher NA beverage sales during 3pm-8pm window compared to control venues; 40% of purchasers under 35; 30% repeat purchase rate within the test period.

---

### Experiment 2: The Tangible Taste Object

**Hypothesis:** Pairing a premium NA beverage with a limited-edition physical object that "translates" its flavour profile will increase willingness to pay by 40% and generate 3x social media engagement compared to standard packaging.

**Method:** Collaborate with an emerging designer to create 500 limited-edition objects (ceramic, glass, or textile) that interpret the flavour profile of a single premium NA beverage. Sell the beverage-plus-object pairing at a 40% premium through a single e-commerce drop. Track sell-through velocity, social media posts featuring the object, secondary market pricing (if any), and customer feedback on the object-flavour relationship. Compare engagement metrics to standard product launches.

**Success metric:** Complete sell-through within 72 hours; 200+ organic social media posts featuring the object; average customer rating of 4.5/5 on "object captures the flavour" question; 15% of purchasers listing item on secondary market within 30 days (indicating collectibility perception).

---

### Experiment 3: The Complexity Confession

**Hypothesis:** NA beverage marketing that acknowledges emotional complexity ("for when you're feeling everything at once") will outperform simple benefit claims ("for calm") among consumers aged 25-40 in both engagement and purchase intent.

**Method:** Develop two parallel social media campaigns for the same NA functional beverage. Campaign A uses traditional single-benefit messaging ("Find your calm"). Campaign B uses complexity-acknowledging messaging ("For sitting with uncertainty," "For feeling it all," "For the in-between moments"). Run both campaigns with identical spend across Instagram and TikTok for 6 weeks, targeting 25-40 demographic. Measure engagement rates, click-through to purchase, and conduct post-exposure surveys measuring brand perception, authenticity, and purchase intent.

**Success metric:** Campaign B achieves 35% higher engagement rate; 20% higher click-through to purchase; scores 25% higher on "this brand understands me" in post-exposure survey; no decrease in clarity of product understanding compared to Campaign A.
