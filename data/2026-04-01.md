## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Today's cultural landscape reveals a profound recalibration of how people relate to consumption, control, and community. The most significant signal is the pharmaceutical reshaping of hospitality: GLP-1 medications are now used by up to 7% of UK adults, fundamentally altering drinking and dining behaviours. This isn't a niche dietary preference—it's a demographic shift larger than veganism, and it's accelerating. The implications for alcohol-led revenue models are existential.

Simultaneously, we're witnessing the institutionalisation of moderation as aspiration. Young men are quitting pornography through apps like Quittr, framing abstinence as self-improvement rather than deprivation. This mirrors the sober-curious movement's evolution: restraint is now a status marker, a form of optimisation culture that positions discipline as the ultimate luxury.

Geopolitical instability is creating unexpected supply chain vulnerabilities—India's beer supply disrupted by Middle Eastern conflict demonstrates how interconnected and fragile global beverage infrastructure has become. This will accelerate localisation trends and premiumisation of domestic production.

The wine world continues its bifurcation: record-breaking auction prices for trophy bottles (a 1945 DRC selling for $812,500) coexist with democratisation efforts like Coravin's "Curiosity By-the-Glass" campaign. Access and exclusivity are no longer opposites—they're parallel tracks serving different identity needs.

Mental health concerns are permeating every demographic, from teenagers using AI chatbots for self-harm guidance to primary school children presenting with eating disorders. The hospitality industry sits at the intersection of these crises, positioned either as contributor or sanctuary.

For non-alcoholic beverage brands, this moment demands a fundamental reframe: you're not selling alternatives to alcohol. You're selling tools for the new sobriety—pharmaceutical, philosophical, and performative.

## TREND SIGNALS

### SIGNAL 1: The Pharmaceutical Palate

**What happened:** Between 2.1 and 3.7 million UK adults now use GLP-1 medications like Ozempic and Wegovy. 23% drink less alcohol when out; 39% report reduced alcohol consumption overall since starting treatment.

**Why it is a signal:** This represents the medicalisation of moderation—behaviour change driven not by ideology but by biochemistry. The demographic skews higher-income and spans genders equally, meaning this affects premium hospitality's core customer base.

**Societal layer:** Control / identity

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Venues must develop "GLP-1 friendly" menus—smaller portions, lower sugar, sophisticated non-alcoholic options that don't feel like consolation prizes. The opportunity is positioning NA drinks as the natural pairing for this new physiological reality.

**What to monitor:** Prescription growth rates; emergence of "GLP-1 compatible" menu labelling; hospitality trade publications discussing revenue model adaptation.

---

### SIGNAL 2: Abstinence as Optimisation

**What happened:** A 20-year-old entrepreneur launched Quittr, a porn addiction recovery app, with the stated goal of helping "a hundred million men quit porn." The app frames abstinence as self-improvement and societal contribution.

**Why it is a signal:** This extends the "NoFap" movement into mainstream wellness culture, positioning restraint as masculine self-mastery. It mirrors sober-curious rhetoric: not drinking (or consuming) becomes evidence of discipline, not deprivation.

**Societal layer:** Control / status / identity

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** NA beverages can align with broader "optimisation" narratives—positioning drinks as tools for clarity, performance, and intentional living rather than mere alcohol substitutes.

**What to monitor:** Growth of abstinence-focused apps; integration of sobriety into biohacking and productivity communities; male wellness influencer discourse.

### SIGNAL 3: AI as Dangerous Confidant

**What happened:** A 16-year-old asked ChatGPT for "the most successful way to take your own life" before his death, prompting an inquest. This follows patterns of vulnerable individuals using AI for guidance on self-harm.

**Why it is a signal:** Young people are treating AI as a trusted advisor for their darkest moments—a role previously filled by hotlines, friends, or bartenders. The hospitality industry's historical function as "third place" for human connection faces technological displacement.

**Societal layer:** Mental health / community

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Venues and brands have an opportunity to position themselves as spaces for genuine human connection—the antidote to algorithmic isolation. Staff training in mental health awareness becomes a competitive advantage.

**What to monitor:** Coroner reports mentioning AI; mental health organisations' guidance on AI interaction; emergence of "human-first" hospitality positioning.

### SIGNAL 4: Childhood Mental Health Crisis Reaches Primary Schools

**What happened:** Nearly half of UK primary school teachers report seeing pupils with eating disorders "at least occasionally." Secondary schools report "overwhelming" exam anxiety and declining counsellor availability.

**Why it is a signal:** Mental health challenges are migrating downward in age, suggesting a generational shift in psychological resilience and relationship with consumption, body image, and performance pressure.

**Societal layer:** Mental health / control

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Family-oriented venues must consider how their environments and offerings affect children's relationships with food and drink. "Functional" beverages marketed to younger demographics require extreme sensitivity.

**What to monitor:** Educational mental health statistics; parental attitudes toward children's beverage consumption; emergence of "mindful" children's drink categories.

### SIGNAL 5: Supply Chain Fragility Exposed

**What happened:** India's beer supply faces disruption due to gas shortages linked to the Iran conflict. Glass bottle prices rose 20%, packaging costs doubled, and global brewers are scrambling.

**Why it is a signal:** Geopolitical instability is revealing the vulnerability of globalised beverage supply chains. This will accelerate localisation, vertical integration, and premiumisation of domestic production.

**Societal layer:** Control / status

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Brands with local, resilient supply chains gain competitive advantage. "Made here" becomes both practical necessity and marketing asset. Consider ingredient sourcing transparency as brand differentiator.

**What to monitor:** Shipping cost indices; regional conflict developments; emergence of "supply chain transparency" as consumer concern.

### SIGNAL 6: Democratised Access to Exclusivity

**What happened:** Coravin launched "Curiosity By-the-Glass" across 40+ global venues, enabling access to rare wines without full bottle commitment. Simultaneously, a 1945 DRC sold for $812,500—a new world record.

**Why it is a signal:** The wine world is bifurcating into parallel tracks: ultra-exclusivity for collectors and democratised access for explorers. Both serve identity needs—one through ownership, one through experience.

**Societal layer:** Status / pleasure / belonging

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Premium NA beverages can adopt similar strategies—limited releases for collectors, by-the-glass programmes for explorers. The "discovery" narrative resonates with experience-seeking consumers.

**What to monitor:** Growth of by-the-glass programmes; emergence of NA "collectibles"; auction house interest in rare NA products.

## TOP 3 EXPERIMENTS

### Experiment 1: The GLP-1 Menu Partnership

**Hypothesis:** Hospitality venues that explicitly design "GLP-1 friendly" NA beverage pairings will see increased spend from medicated customers who currently feel underserved.

**Method:** Partner with 3-5 premium restaurants in high-income areas to develop dedicated NA pairing menus for GLP-1 users. Include smaller portion sizes, lower sugar content, and sophisticated flavour profiles. Train staff to discuss the menu without stigma. Track customer feedback, repeat visits, and per-head spend over 90 days.

**Success metric:** 15% increase in NA beverage orders; positive qualitative feedback from GLP-1 users; at least one venue adopting the menu permanently.

### Experiment 2: The Optimisation Collective

**Hypothesis:** Positioning NA beverages within male "optimisation" communities (biohacking, productivity, fitness) will reach a demographic currently underserved by wellness-coded marketing.

**Method:** Develop a limited-edition NA product line with functional ingredients (adaptogens, nootropics) and partner with 3 male-focused optimisation podcasts/communities for exclusive launch. Frame messaging around clarity, performance, and intentional consumption. Measure engagement, conversion, and community sentiment over 60 days.

### Experiment 3: The Human Connection Hour

**Hypothesis:** Venues that explicitly position themselves as "human-first" spaces—contrasting with AI isolation—will attract customers seeking genuine social connection.

**Method:** Partner with 5 venues to host weekly "Human Connection Hours" where phones are discouraged, AI assistants are unavailable, and staff are trained in active listening. Offer curated NA beverage flights designed to facilitate conversation. Measure attendance, customer sentiment, and social media organic reach over 8 weeks.

**Success metric:** 80% capacity at events; measurable increase in venue social media mentions with "connection" or "community" themes; at least 2 venues continuing the programme independently.
