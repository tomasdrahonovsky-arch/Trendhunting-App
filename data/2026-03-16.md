## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Today's cultural landscape reveals a society grappling with fundamental questions of care, autonomy, and authenticity—themes that directly shape how people choose what to consume and where to gather. The most significant pattern emerging is a recalibration of what constitutes genuine value versus performative virtue.

Britain's charitable giving has collapsed by £1.4bn, with only half the population now donating—down from 61% a decade ago. This isn't simply economic pressure; it signals a deeper shift in how people express social responsibility. Simultaneously, a landmark UK study shows that unconditional cash payments to care leavers improved housing stability, relationships, and reduced substance use—challenging paternalistic assumptions about how support should be structured.

The tension between institutional control and individual autonomy surfaces repeatedly: Google abandoned its AI health advice feature after crowdsourcing amateur medical guidance, while women continue to be arrested for suspected illegal abortions despite parliament voting to decriminalise. People are increasingly sceptical of systems that claim to know what's best for them.

In food and drink, we see a rejection of excess in favour of restraint and terroir. Finger Lakes winemakers are lobbying for Cabernet Franc—a cool-climate, lower-alcohol varietal—to become New York's official grape, explicitly positioning against "super-high-alcohol, fleshy reds." This mirrors broader consumer fatigue with maximalism.

Meanwhile, research on infant screen time and baby deception reveals society's anxious preoccupation with developmental purity and authenticity from the earliest age—a concern that extends to how adults evaluate their own consumption choices.

For non-alcoholic beverage brands, the strategic imperative is clear: consumers want products and experiences that respect their intelligence, offer genuine rather than performed wellness, and acknowledge complexity rather than promising simple solutions.

## TREND SIGNALS

### SIGNAL 1: The Collapse of Performative Generosity

**What happened:** British charitable donations fell by £1.4bn in 2025, with only 50% of people giving to charity compared to 61% a decade ago. The Charities Aid Foundation explicitly states giving is no longer a "deeply embedded cultural norm."

**Why it is a signal:** This represents a fundamental shift from collective obligation to selective, personal impact. People aren't necessarily less generous—they're more sceptical of institutional intermediaries and want direct evidence their contribution matters.

**Societal layer:** Community / belonging

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Cause-marketing fatigue is real. Brands claiming to "give back" without transparency will face cynicism. Consider hyper-local, verifiable impact—supporting a named farmer, a specific community initiative—rather than abstract charitable partnerships.

**What to monitor:** Growth of mutual aid networks; direct-to-beneficiary platforms; consumer response to "impact receipts" showing exactly where money goes.

---

### SIGNAL 2: Unconditional Trust as Intervention

**What happened:** A King's College London study found care leavers given £2,000 with no strings attached were more likely to be in stable housing, happier, and spent less on alcohol, tobacco, and drugs than control groups.

**Why it is a signal:** This challenges the paternalistic assumption that vulnerable people need guidance on how to spend resources. Trust, not control, produced better outcomes—including reduced substance use.

**Societal layer:** Control / autonomy

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** The wellness beverage space often adopts a prescriptive tone ("drink this for gut health"). Consider positioning that trusts consumers to know what they need, offering information without moralising. Autonomy-respecting messaging may resonate more than directive health claims.

**What to monitor:** Policy experiments with universal basic income; consumer backlash against "should" marketing; growth of choose-your-own-benefit subscription models.

---

### SIGNAL 3: The Elegance of Restraint in Wine

**What happened:** Finger Lakes winemakers are lobbying for Cabernet Franc to become New York's official grape, explicitly positioning cool-climate, lower-alcohol wines against "super-high-alcohol, fleshy reds" from regions like Napa.

**Why it is a signal:** This represents a cultural pivot from maximalism to restraint—from wines that overwhelm to wines that require attention. It's terroir as identity, not just taste.

**Societal layer:** Status / identity

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** The "sessionable" positioning for NA drinks gains cultural legitimacy. Emphasise complexity and subtlety over intensity. Consider regional NA beverages that express place—botanical blends tied to specific terroirs, positioned as sophisticated alternatives to bold flavour profiles.

**What to monitor:** Growth of low-intervention, lower-ABV wine categories; consumer language around "drinkability" versus "impact"; emergence of NA terroir claims.

---

### SIGNAL 4: Institutional AI Retreat on Health

**What happened:** Google scrapped its "What People Suggest" AI feature that crowdsourced amateur health advice, after launching it as showing "the potential of AI to transform health outcomes across the globe."

**Why it is a signal:** Even tech giants are recognising that democratised health information can be dangerous. This creates space for trusted, expert-backed wellness claims while simultaneously increasing consumer scepticism of all health messaging.

**Societal layer:** Control / mental health

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** The functional beverage space must navigate heightened scrutiny of health claims. Third-party validation, clinical backing, and transparent sourcing become competitive advantages. Avoid vague wellness language; specificity builds trust.

**What to monitor:** Regulatory tightening on functional claims; consumer demand for clinical evidence; growth of "doctor-developed" or "nutritionist-approved" positioning.

---

### SIGNAL 5: GLP-1 Drugs and the Craving Economy

**What happened:** Research suggests weight-loss drugs (GLP-1 agonists) may reduce cravings broadly—not just for food, but for addictive substances. Patients on these drugs showed roughly half the overdose or drug-related death rates.

**Why it is a signal:** If pharmaceutical interventions can modulate desire itself, the entire landscape of indulgence, moderation, and "treating yourself" shifts. What does pleasure mean when craving can be chemically managed?

**Societal layer:** Pleasure / control

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** A growing segment may experience fundamentally altered relationships with consumption. Products positioned around "satisfying cravings" may need to evolve toward "enhancing experience" for consumers whose baseline desire has changed. Consider beverages designed for people who drink less but want each drink to matter more.

**What to monitor:** GLP-1 prescription rates; consumer forums discussing changed relationships with food/drink; emergence of "post-craving" lifestyle content.

---

### SIGNAL 6: The Authenticity Anxiety of Early Childhood

**What happened:** Two studies surfaced: 75% of nine-month-olds have daily screen time, and research suggests babies practice deception before age one—hiding toys, pretending not to hear parents.

**Why it is a signal:** Society's obsession with developmental purity and authentic selfhood now extends to infancy. This reflects broader anxiety about when and how we become "corrupted" by modern life—and a desire to return to some imagined natural state.

**Societal layer:** Identity / spirituality

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** "Clean" and "pure" positioning resonates because it taps into this anxiety about contamination. However, sophistication lies in acknowledging complexity—products that are "honest about what they are" rather than claiming impossible purity may build deeper trust with discerning consumers.

**What to monitor:** Parenting discourse around "natural" development; growth of "nothing artificial" claims; consumer response to brands that acknowledge trade-offs.

---

### SIGNAL 7: Sparkling Wine's Punk Moment

**What happened:** Finger Lakes producers report that "anything sparkling is doing well"—including unconventional expressions like sparkling Riesling, Cabernet Franc, and Saperavi. One winemaker describes it as "punk rock" sparkling wine.

**Why it is a signal:** Sparkling wine is being liberated from occasion and tradition. It's becoming a format for experimentation rather than a category defined by Champagne rules.

**Societal layer:** Rebellion / pleasure

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Carbonation as a canvas for experimentation, not just a delivery mechanism. NA sparkling beverages can push further into unexpected flavour territories—savoury, tannic, herbal—without being constrained by what "sparkling" traditionally means.

**What to monitor:** Growth of pétillant naturel and unconventional sparkling wines; consumer willingness to try "weird" carbonated beverages; emergence of sparkling as everyday rather than celebratory.

---

### SIGNAL 8: The Persistence of Bodily Autonomy Battles

**What happened:** Despite parliament voting to decriminalise abortion, women in England continue to be arrested for suspected illegal terminations. The gap between legislative intent and enforcement reality persists.

**Why it is a signal:** Institutional change doesn't guarantee cultural or operational change. People increasingly recognise that stated values and actual practices diverge—breeding scepticism toward all institutional claims.

**Societal layer:** Control / autonomy

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Brand values must be operationalised, not just stated. Consumers will investigate whether sustainability claims, ethical sourcing, or wellness positioning are reflected in actual practice. The gap between marketing and reality is increasingly visible and punished.

**What to monitor:** Growth of brand accountability journalism; consumer use of supply chain transparency tools; social media "receipts" culture applied to brands.

---

### SIGNAL 9: Sleep as Medical Frontier

**What happened:** CPAP machines, once reserved for severe sleep apnoea, are increasingly prescribed for milder cases. Sleep medicine is expanding its clinical footprint.

**Why it is a signal:** Sleep is being medicalised and optimised at scale. This extends the "quantified self" movement into previously private, passive time—and creates new anxiety about whether one is sleeping "correctly."

**Societal layer:** Mental health / control

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Sleep-support beverages have significant runway, but must navigate the tension between relaxation and optimisation. Consider positioning that emphasises rest as valuable in itself, not just as performance enhancement. Evening rituals and wind-down occasions represent underserved moments.

**What to monitor:** Growth of sleep clinics and diagnostics; consumer adoption of sleep tracking; emergence of "sleep hygiene" as mainstream concern.

---

### SIGNAL 10: The Tradition Defence Reflex

**What happened:** Felicity Cloake's Irish stew recipe explicitly warns against modern deviation: "It's tempting for modern cooks to meddle with such resolutely plain classics. Do not!"

**Why it is a signal:** Amid constant innovation, there's a counter-movement defending tradition as complete in itself. Not everything needs improvement or personalisation—some things are finished.

**Societal layer:** Belonging / identity

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Heritage and "original recipe" positioning gains power as a counterweight to endless innovation. Consider products that explicitly refuse to trend-chase—"we've made this the same way since [year]" as a feature, not a limitation.

**What to monitor:** Growth of "heritage" and "traditional" claims; consumer fatigue with limited editions and collaborations; nostalgia marketing effectiveness.

## TOP 3 EXPERIMENTS

### Experiment 1: The Terroir Tonic Series

**Hypothesis:** Consumers seeking sophisticated NA alternatives will pay premium prices for botanical beverages explicitly tied to specific regional terroirs, positioned as the NA equivalent of cool-climate wines.

**Method:** Develop three limited-edition NA tonics, each sourced from and named after a specific microregion (e.g., "Mendocino Coastal Botanical," "Hudson Valley Orchard Blend"). Partner with a single farm or forager per expression. Include detailed terroir notes on packaging. Price at £8-12 per bottle. Distribute through wine shops and upscale hospitality venues in test markets.

**Success metric:** 40% sell-through within 60 days; qualitative feedback indicating consumers understand and value the terroir positioning; repeat purchase rate above 25%.

---

### Experiment 2: The Unconditional Welcome Programme

**Hypothesis:** Hospitality venues that explicitly trust guests—removing friction, surveillance, and prescriptive guidance—will generate stronger loyalty and word-of-mouth than venues with extensive rules and recommendations.

**Method:** Partner with three bars/cafes to implement an "unconditional welcome" pilot: remove dress codes, eliminate "suggested pairings," train staff to offer information only when asked, and create spaces where guests set their own pace. Measure dwell time, return visits, and Net Promoter Score against control venues with standard service models.

**Success metric:** 20% increase in return visits; NPS improvement of 15+ points; qualitative feedback citing "feeling trusted" or "no pressure" as key differentiators.

---

### Experiment 3: The Post-Craving Tasting Format

**Hypothesis:** A growing segment of consumers on GLP-1 medications or otherwise experiencing reduced cravings will respond to tasting experiences designed around intensity and memorability rather than volume or satisfaction.

**Method:** Create a "Micro-Tasting" format: five 30ml pours of complex NA beverages, each paired with a sensory prompt (aroma card, texture sample, sound). Position as "designed for people who drink less but want each sip to matter." Test at three wellness-adjacent venues. Survey participants about medication/supplement use (optional, anonymous) to understand audience composition.

**Success metric:** 70% of participants rate experience as "memorable" or "worth repeating"; identification of GLP-1 user segment within audience; development of replicable format for broader rollout.
