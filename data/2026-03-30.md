## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Today's cultural landscape reveals a profound tension between heritage authenticity and modern accessibility, playing out across beverages, hospitality, and identity formation. The most significant signal is the strategic repositioning of traditional spirits categories—baijiu, sake, sherry, craft cider—not through dilution of their essence, but through new contexts of discovery. Cocktail bars are becoming cultural embassies; travel retail is functioning as a global tasting room; Michelin-starred restaurants are evangelising regional drinking traditions.

Simultaneously, the non-alcoholic space is maturing beyond novelty. Constellation Brands' full acquisition of Hopwtr signals that hop water has graduated from venture experiment to core portfolio strategy. This isn't about replacing beer—it's about capturing the broader "mindful drinker" demographic that transcends any single category. The functional benefit proposition (adaptogens, nootropics) combined with beer-adjacent sensory cues represents a template for the next generation of adult beverages.

Sustainability discourse is entering a more rigorous phase. The London Wine Fair's "Bullsh!t Bingo" session on greenwashing signals industry fatigue with performative environmentalism. Wales' deposit return scheme, diverging from UK-wide policy, creates regulatory complexity but also demonstrates that regional governments will act unilaterally when national coordination stalls.

The cultural undercurrent connecting these threads: consumers increasingly seek beverages that carry meaning—whether through provenance, ritual, functional benefit, or environmental integrity. The era of passive consumption is closing. What's opening is a market where drinks must earn their place through story, substance, and alignment with evolving values around moderation, authenticity, and planetary responsibility. Brands that understand this shift will build lasting relevance; those that don't will find themselves competing solely on price.

## TREND SIGNALS

### SIGNAL 1: Cocktail Bars as Cultural Embassies for Traditional Spirits

**What happened:** Baijiu bar Sanyou in Guangzhou hosted a cocktail party for Chinese spirits executives and global bartenders, including Oslo's Himkok (ranked 14th globally). Major baijiu brands were "surprised to see the interest and curiosity the world has for baijiu."

**Why it is a signal:** Traditional spirits categories facing domestic decline are discovering that cocktail culture provides a bridge to younger, global consumers. The bartender becomes the translator between heritage and contemporary taste.

**Societal layer:** Status / belonging

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Non-alcoholic brands with cultural depth (botanical heritage, regional provenance) should prioritise bartender education and cocktail integration. The on-premise channel functions as a credibility gateway before retail expansion.

**What to monitor:** Bartender-focused training programmes from heritage brands; cocktail competition categories featuring traditional spirits; emergence of "cultural cocktail bars" in Western cities.

### SIGNAL 2: Hop Water Graduates to Core Portfolio Strategy

**What happened:** Constellation Brands acquired Hopwtr outright after five years in its venture portfolio, citing the brand's ability to capture "mindful drinkers" beyond just moderating beer consumers.

**Why it is a signal:** This moves hop water from experimental adjacency to strategic priority. The category's appeal lies in its broader demographic reach—it's not cannibalising beer but creating new occasions for beer-curious non-drinkers.

**Societal layer:** Control / pleasure

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Functional benefits (adaptogens, nootropics) combined with familiar sensory cues represent the winning formula. Hospitality venues should consider hop water as a distinct category, not a beer substitute, with its own menu positioning.

**What to monitor:** Major beverage conglomerate M&A in functional non-alc space; hop water tap handles in craft beer bars; consumer research on "zebra striping" behaviour patterns.

### SIGNAL 3: Craft Cider's Quiet Ascent Through Community Building

**What happened:** The Three Counties Cider and Perry Association's CraftCon drew its largest attendance ever, with nearly 200 visitors from seven countries. Craft cider now represents 12% of UK draught volume but 14% of value share.

**Why it is a signal:** Craft cider is following the premiumisation playbook that craft beer established, but with stronger terroir narratives and heritage positioning. The international attendance suggests global appetite for regional authenticity.

**Societal layer:** Community / belonging

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Non-alcoholic cider remains underdeveloped relative to non-alc beer and wine. The craft cider community's emphasis on "stories" and "human hands" provides a template for NA cider positioning that emphasises orchard provenance over alcohol removal.

**What to monitor:** Non-alcoholic cider launches from established craft producers; wassailing and orchard tourism as experience categories; cider's presence in natural wine bar programmes.

### SIGNAL 4: Greenwashing Fatigue Demands Rigorous Sustainability Claims

**What happened:** The London Wine Fair is hosting "Bullsh!t Bingo: Calling Out Greenwashing in Drinks Communications" as part of an expanded sustainability programme with dedicated hub and measurement frameworks.

**Why it is a signal:** The industry is moving from sustainability as marketing narrative to sustainability as auditable practice. Performative environmentalism is becoming a reputational liability rather than an asset.

**Societal layer:** Identity / control

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** NA brands that have leaned heavily on "natural" or "clean" positioning without substantiation face credibility risk. Investment in third-party verification and transparent impact reporting becomes competitive advantage.

**What to monitor:** Regulatory developments on environmental claims in beverages; emergence of sustainability certification standards specific to drinks; consumer research on greenwashing scepticism by demographic.

### SIGNAL 5: Regional Regulatory Divergence Creates Complexity and Opportunity

**What happened:** Wales approved its own deposit return scheme for drinks containers, diverging from UK-wide policy and prompting industry concern about "interoperability" while the Welsh Government frames it as circular economy leadership.

**Why it is a signal:** Regional governments are willing to act unilaterally on environmental policy when national coordination stalls. This creates operational complexity but also first-mover positioning opportunities for brands that adapt quickly.

**Societal layer:** Control / community

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Brands with UK distribution must now plan for regulatory fragmentation. Those that embrace regional schemes early can build goodwill with environmentally-conscious consumers in those markets.

**What to monitor:** Scotland's DRS implementation progress; English policy response to Welsh divergence; brand communication strategies around regional environmental compliance.

### SIGNAL 6: Sake's UK Momentum Builds Through Versatility Positioning

**What happened:** Akashi-Tai sake sales are up 26% year-on-year in the UK, with the brand emphasising cocktail collaborations, wine glass service, and attributes (gluten-free, vegan, no added sugar) that align with moderation trends.

**Why it is a signal:** Sake is successfully repositioning from niche Japanese restaurant accompaniment to versatile, health-conscious adult beverage. Its relatively low ABV naturally aligns with moderation without requiring reformulation.

**Societal layer:** Pleasure / status

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Sake's success suggests that "lower ABV" can be a stepping stone category for consumers moving toward non-alcoholic options. Hospitality venues should consider sake as part of a moderation-friendly programme alongside NA offerings.

**What to monitor:** Sake cocktail menu penetration in non-Japanese restaurants; sparkling sake as Champagne alternative positioning; sake brand investment in UK market education.

### SIGNAL 9: EU's Hard Line on Wine GIs in Trade Negotiations

**What happened:** In EU-Australia trade negotiations, the EU offered controlled agricultural access (beef quotas with conditions) while maintaining non-negotiable protection for wine geographical indications like Champagne and Prosecco.

**Why it is a signal:** Wine's protected status in trade policy reflects its cultural and economic significance to European identity. This creates both barriers and opportunities for non-European producers seeking to build regional authenticity narratives.

**Societal layer:** Identity / belonging

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** NA brands building regional identity claims should study GI protection frameworks. As the category matures, expect disputes over terms like "Champagne-style" or regional botanical claims in non-alcoholic products.

**What to monitor:** GI enforcement actions in beverages; NA brand naming conventions and regional claims; trade policy developments affecting beverage categories.

## TOP 3 EXPERIMENTS

### Experiment 1: Bartender Ambassador Programme for Heritage-Forward NA Botanicals

**Hypothesis:** Non-alcoholic brands with strong provenance stories (specific botanical origins, traditional preparation methods) can accelerate on-premise adoption by replicating the "cultural embassy" model that baijiu and sake are using—investing in bartender education and cocktail development rather than consumer marketing.

**Method:** Partner with 10-15 cocktail bars known for spirits education (similar to Sanyou's approach). Provide intensive training on botanical sourcing, production methods, and flavour profiles. Co-develop 3-5 signature NA cocktails per venue. Measure bartender advocacy through social mentions, menu placement, and unprompted recommendations.

**Success metric:** 40% of participating bartenders actively recommend the NA product without prompting within 90 days; 25% increase in on-premise velocity at participating venues versus control group.

---

### Experiment 2: "Verified Impact" Sustainability Certification for NA Beverages

**Hypothesis:** As greenwashing fatigue intensifies, NA brands that invest in third-party verified sustainability claims will outperform those relying on self-reported "natural" or "clean" positioning, particularly among consumers under 35.

**Method:** Develop or adopt a rigorous sustainability certification framework covering carbon footprint, water usage, packaging lifecycle, and supply chain transparency. Launch with 3-5 NA brands willing to undergo full audit. Create consumer-facing communication comparing verified claims to industry standard vague language. A/B test purchase intent and willingness-to-pay.

**Success metric:** 15% higher purchase intent for verified products versus non-verified competitors; 20% premium price acceptance among target demographic.

---

### Experiment 3: Travel Retail Discovery Programme for Premium NA

**Hypothesis:** Airport retail can function as a high-impact discovery channel for premium NA beverages, reaching consumers in an exploratory mindset with reduced price sensitivity—replicating Ukiyo's Japanese gin strategy.

**Method:** Negotiate trial placement in 5-10 international airport retail locations through a single travel retail operator. Position products in "discovery" or "wellness" sections rather than traditional beverage aisles. Offer tasting samples during peak travel periods. Track purchase conversion, repeat purchase (via loyalty programme data), and social media mentions tagged with travel context.

**Success metric:** 8% conversion rate from tasting to purchase; 30% of purchasers mention product on social media within 7 days; evidence of home market purchase following travel discovery.
