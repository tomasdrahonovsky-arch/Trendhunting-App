## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Today's cultural landscape reveals a market in active recalibration—consumers and industries alike are navigating between resilience and retreat, authenticity and performance, tradition and reinvention. The global beverage alcohol market's 2% volume decline masks more nuanced shifts: premiumisation continues despite economic headwinds, occasion-driven consumption patterns are hardening, and the search for meaning through craft, community, and cultural exchange intensifies.

Three dominant tensions emerge. First, the **authenticity paradox**: as celebrity-fronted wine launches proliferate (Valeria Mazza's Finca Valeria) and international wine regions forge identity-sharing pacts (Langhe-Yoichi), consumers simultaneously crave genuine provenance while accepting curated lifestyle narratives. Second, **community as infrastructure**: from charity bike rides honouring industry figures to smutty book clubs in Cambridge pubs, people are building micro-communities around shared passions that transcend traditional social boundaries. Third, **the dealcoholisation of premium**: Torres' Serena Mode 0.0 Cabernet Sauvignon signals that alcohol-free is no longer a compromise category but a legitimate expression of winemaking craft.

The hospitality sector faces a supply-side reckoning with Encirc's bottle production strikes threatening major brands, while simultaneously confronting demand-side fragmentation as consumers retreat into niche interest groups. The cultural moment demands brands that can hold complexity—offering both the comfort of tradition and the excitement of reinvention, the intimacy of community and the aspiration of premium positioning.

For non-alcoholic beverage strategists, the opportunity lies in recognising that today's consumer isn't choosing between alcohol and abstinence—they're choosing between experiences that feel meaningful and those that feel transactional. The winners will be those who understand that premiumisation is less about price point and more about emotional resonance.

## TREND SIGNALS

### SIGNAL 1: The Premiumisation Paradox in Decline

**What happened:** IWSR data reveals global beverage alcohol volume dropped 2% across 22 markets in 2025, yet rosé continues to outperform as "one of the clearest areas of growth and resilience," with Félix Solís launching a premium lifestyle-led range specifically targeting this segment.

**Why it is a signal:** Consumers aren't drinking less because they want less—they're drinking less but better. The bifurcation between volume decline and premium segment resilience indicates a fundamental shift in how people relate to beverages as identity markers rather than mere refreshment.

**Societal layer:** Status / Identity

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Premium NA beverages must compete on the same emotional and aesthetic terrain as premium alcohol. The "occasion-driven" consumption pattern cited for rosé's success applies equally to sophisticated NA options—positioning around moments, not abstinence.

**What to monitor:** Premium NA pricing acceptance thresholds; rosé-adjacent NA product launches; lifestyle brand collaborations in the NA space.

### SIGNAL 2: Cross-Cultural Craft Alliances

**What happened:** Italy's Langhe region and Japan's Yoichi district signed a "wine pact" sealed by dipping thumbs in red wine—a collaboration founded on shared values of sustainability, climate adaptation, and pursuit of excellence rather than commercial transaction.

**Why it is a signal:** This represents a new model of cultural exchange where regions trade expertise rather than products. The ritual nature of the signing (blood-red fingerprints on white documents) signals that authenticity now requires performative demonstration of commitment.

**Societal layer:** Belonging / Spirituality

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** NA brands could forge similar "craft alliances" with unexpected partners—tea regions, botanical gardens, or wellness traditions from different cultures. The key is shared philosophy, not just ingredient sourcing.

**What to monitor:** Other cross-regional craft partnerships; consumer response to "origin story" complexity; the emergence of "terroir" language in NA categories.

### SIGNAL 3: The Dealcoholisation of Prestige

**What happened:** Miguel Torres Chile launched Serena Mode Cabernet Sauvignon 0.0, explicitly designed to "retain varietal character without artificial flavourings," targeting "premium alcohol-free red wines" as a legitimate category.

**Why it is a signal:** A heritage winemaker treating dealcoholised wine with the same craft language as traditional wine marks a categorical shift. This isn't about health compromise—it's about expanding what "premium" means.

**Societal layer:** Status / Pleasure

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** The language of craft, terroir, and varietal expression is now available to NA beverages. Hospitality venues should consider NA wine lists with the same curatorial attention as alcoholic selections.

**What to monitor:** Sommelier adoption of NA wines; premium restaurant NA pairing menus; wine critic coverage of dealcoholised products.

### SIGNAL 4: Community Grief as Brand Building

**What happened:** The UK drinks industry is organising a 60-mile charity cycle from London to Brighton honouring the late John Osborne, with vineyard stops and a finish at his beloved football team's stadium—explicitly combining fundraising with mental health awareness.

**Why it is a signal:** Industry communities are creating ritualised experiences around loss that strengthen professional bonds while addressing previously taboo topics like mental health. This represents a maturation of "industry culture" beyond networking into genuine mutual support.

**Societal layer:** Community / Mental Health

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** NA brands have an opportunity to sponsor or create community wellness events that acknowledge the industry's relationship with alcohol while offering alternatives. The combination of physical challenge, charitable purpose, and social connection is a template.

**What to monitor:** Industry mental health initiatives; wellness-oriented trade events; the emergence of "sober curious" professional networks.

### SIGNAL 5: Supply Chain Vulnerability as Category Risk

**What happened:** Encirc glass workers announced escalating strikes through May, threatening bottle supply for major brands including Jameson, Baileys, Budweiser, and supermarket wines. The union claims "significant impact" on supply chains.

**Why it is a signal:** The concentration of glass production creates systemic vulnerability that could accelerate alternative packaging adoption. Labour disputes in manufacturing expose the hidden infrastructure dependencies of beverage categories.

**Societal layer:** Control

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** NA brands should diversify packaging strategies now—cans, cartons, and bag-in-box formats may gain legitimacy faster if glass shortages materialise. First movers in premium alternative packaging could capture market share.

**What to monitor:** Glass pricing and availability; consumer acceptance of alternative packaging for premium products; competitor packaging pivots.

**Societal layer:** Pleasure / Belonging / Rebellion

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Venues and brands can create or sponsor interest-based communities that combine intellectual engagement with sensory pleasure. NA beverages positioned as "indulgent" rather than "virtuous" may resonate with this demographic.

**What to monitor:** Niche interest club formation; venue programming trends; the intersection of "guilty pleasures" and social acceptability.

## TOP 3 EXPERIMENTS

### Experiment 1: The Craft Alliance Programme

**Hypothesis:** NA beverage brands that forge visible "craft alliances" with unexpected cultural partners (tea estates, botanical gardens, wellness traditions) will achieve higher perceived authenticity and premium positioning than those relying solely on ingredient sourcing claims.

**Method:** Partner with a heritage institution (e.g., Kew Gardens, a Japanese tea ceremony school, or a Mediterranean herb cooperative) to co-develop a limited-edition NA beverage. Document the collaboration through ritual-rich content (similar to the Langhe-Yoichi thumb-print ceremony). Launch through hospitality partners who can tell the story. Compare brand perception metrics against a control product launched with standard provenance messaging.

**Success metric:** 25% higher "authenticity" scores in consumer research; 40% premium price acceptance vs. control; earned media coverage in lifestyle (not just trade) publications.

---

### Experiment 2: The Pleasure Club Activation

**Hypothesis:** NA beverages positioned as "indulgent" and integrated into pleasure-seeking social formats (book clubs, tasting societies, hobby groups) will outperform those positioned around health or abstinence in driving repeat purchase and brand advocacy.

**Method:** Sponsor or create three "pleasure club" formats in target markets: a smutty book club, a dessert appreciation society, and a sensory exploration group. Provide bespoke NA beverages designed for each context (rich, complex profiles rather than "clean" or "light"). Track participant purchase behaviour, social sharing, and Net Promoter Score against a control group exposed to health-positioned messaging for the same products.

**Success metric:** 50% higher repeat purchase rate among pleasure club participants; 3x social sharing vs. control; NPS above 60.

---

### Experiment 3: The Alternative Packaging Premium Test

**Hypothesis:** Premium NA beverages in non-glass packaging (aluminium, carton, bag-in-box) can achieve equivalent perceived quality to glass when supported by appropriate design investment and sustainability messaging, while reducing supply chain vulnerability.

**Method:** Develop identical NA wine or spirit products in three packaging formats: glass bottle, premium aluminium, and design-forward carton. Conduct blind and sighted tastings with target consumers, measuring quality perception, willingness to pay, and gifting appropriateness. Test in both retail and on-premise contexts. Track any supply disruptions during the Encirc strike period as natural experiment validation.

**Success metric:** Less than 10% quality perception gap between glass and best alternative format; equivalent willingness to pay; 30% of hospitality partners willing to list non-glass format.
