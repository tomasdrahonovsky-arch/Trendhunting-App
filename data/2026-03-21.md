## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Today's cultural landscape reveals a drinks industry in active recalibration—not retreating from alcohol, but demanding more sophisticated choices across the entire spectrum. The dominant narrative of "Gen Z doesn't drink" is being complicated by richer data: wine collectors aren't participating in Dry January, but they are exploring more diverse regions than ever. Meanwhile, younger consumers aren't abstaining wholesale—they're expecting non-alcoholic options to feel like genuine adult choices rather than compromises.

The Asia-Pacific region emerges as a critical battleground where trust and long-term presence matter more than trend-chasing. Vinexpo Asia's three-in-one format (wine, spirits, and dedicated non-alcoholic "Be No" section) signals institutional recognition that no/low has earned its place alongside traditional categories at the highest levels of trade.

Premiumisation continues its march across categories. UK cider is growing through premium draught taps and flavoured innovation. Japanese whisky commands £25,000 per bottle at Gordon Ramsay's Lucky Cat. Champagne houses release exceptional warm vintages. The through-line: consumers will pay more for products with genuine provenance, craft credentials, and compelling narratives.

Cultural infrastructure is shifting to accommodate these preferences. Netflix's Napa Valley drama "Uncorked" represents entertainment capital flowing toward wine storytelling. Côte Brasserie launches a French-only online wine shop. Trade shows restructure around business efficiency rather than volume.

The wellness-adjacent space shows continued appetite for functional benefits—from sauna culture's boom (640+ UK locations) to olive oil-lemon shots on TikTok—suggesting consumers are actively seeking rituals that deliver tangible physical and social benefits. For non-alcoholic beverages, the opportunity lies in meeting this moment with products that deliver on flavour, occasion, and identity—not just absence.

## TREND SIGNALS

### SIGNAL 1: The Diversification Paradox

**What happened:** CellarTracker data reveals wine collectors aren't drinking less—they're drinking more diversely. Dry January "doesn't exist" among engaged enthusiasts, but Savagnin and Pinot Meunier are now favourite varieties, and English producer Simpsons Wine Estate is among fastest-rising by bottle purchases.

**Why it is a signal:** This challenges the binary "drinking vs. not drinking" narrative. Sophisticated consumers are expanding their repertoire rather than contracting it, suggesting curiosity and exploration remain powerful drivers.

**Societal layer:** Identity / Status

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Non-alcoholic brands should position as part of an expanded repertoire, not a replacement. The same consumer buying obscure Jura wines may also want a sophisticated NA option for Tuesday nights. Emphasise discovery and connoisseurship.

**What to monitor:** CellarTracker's NA category additions; specialty retailers stocking NA alongside rare wines; sommelier-curated NA selections.

---

### SIGNAL 2: The "Grown-Up Choice" Imperative

**What happened:** Asahi's research shows 89% of millennials and 92% of Gen Z are open to flavoured non-alcoholic beer, but only if it feels like a "grown-up choice, not a compromise." Peroni 0.0% responds with Limone di Sicilia and Arancia Rossa variants.

**Why it is a signal:** The barrier isn't taste alone—it's perception. Younger consumers reject anything that codes as deprivation or second-class. They want full participation in adult social rituals without alcohol.

**Societal layer:** Identity / Belonging

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Packaging, naming, and occasion positioning matter as much as liquid quality. Avoid "free-from" language. Emphasise what the product delivers, not what it lacks. Premium glassware and dedicated tap handles in venues signal legitimacy.

**What to monitor:** On-premise presentation standards; language shifts in marketing copy; premium price acceptance for NA options.

---

### SIGNAL 3: Institutional Legitimisation of No/Low

**What happened:** Vinexpo Asia 2026 introduces "Be No" as a dedicated section alongside wine and spirits, mirroring the format debuted at Wine Paris. ProWein Düsseldorf features "ProWein Zero" with its own Tasting Zone.

**Why it is a signal:** Major trade shows are the industry's institutional infrastructure. Dedicated space signals that no/low has graduated from novelty to permanent category requiring serious trade attention.

**Societal layer:** Status / Belonging

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** NA brands should prioritise trade show presence and B2B relationship building. Buyers from major import markets are now actively seeking NA products. Distribution partnerships formed at these events will shape retail availability for years.

**What to monitor:** Exhibitor growth in dedicated NA sections; hosted buyer programme inclusion of NA-focused retailers; trade publication coverage ratios.

---

### SIGNAL 4: The Sauna as Third Place

**What happened:** UK sauna locations have grown to 640+ (up from 540 at year's start), with projections suggesting Britain could become the world's largest sauna market by 2033, surpassing Finland and Germany.

**Why it is a signal:** This represents a hunger for embodied, communal, device-free experiences. Saunas offer ritual, physical sensation, and human connection—precisely what digital life lacks.

**Societal layer:** Community / Pleasure / Mental Health

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Sauna venues represent an emerging channel for functional and hydration-focused beverages. Post-sauna rituals could include NA aperitifs or adaptogenic drinks. Partner with sauna operators for exclusive product placement.

**What to monitor:** Sauna venue beverage menus; wellness retreat programming; Nordic-inspired hospitality concepts in UK/US markets.

---

### SIGNAL 5: Entertainment Capital Flows to Wine Narrative

**What happened:** Netflix greenlights "Uncorked," a Darren Star dramedy set in Napa Valley about a "gifted but troubled female winemaker." The streaming platform explicitly frames it as connecting "audiences around the world" to wine culture.

**Why it is a signal:** When entertainment capital invests in category storytelling, it shapes cultural perception at scale. Wine gets the "Emily in Paris" treatment—aspirational, accessible, narrative-driven.

**Societal layer:** Status / Pleasure

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** NA brands lack equivalent cultural storytelling infrastructure. Opportunity exists for documentary content, influencer partnerships, or branded entertainment that makes NA drinking aspirational rather than medicinal.

**What to monitor:** Streaming platform interest in beverage-adjacent content; social media engagement with wine/spirits lifestyle content; NA brand entertainment partnerships.

---

### SIGNAL 6: Flavour as Category Growth Engine

**What happened:** Both Asahi (Peroni 0.0% flavours) and Westons Cider (flavoured NA cider growing to £52.9m, 5.8% of category) identify flavour innovation as the primary mechanism for expanding category penetration.

**Why it is a signal:** Flavour variants attract new consumers who wouldn't try the base product. This mirrors soft drinks' playbook and suggests NA beverages are entering a mature innovation phase.

**Societal layer:** Pleasure / Identity

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Limited-edition and seasonal flavours can drive trial and repeat purchase. Collaborate with chefs or mixologists on unexpected flavour profiles. Use flavour as a storytelling vehicle for provenance and craft.

**What to monitor:** SKU proliferation rates; limited-edition sell-through velocity; flavour-driven social media engagement.

---

### SIGNAL 7: Geopolitical Supply Chain Fragility

**What happened:** Following US/Israel airstrikes on Iran, drinks companies are introducing force majeure clauses. Closure manufacturer Herti UK notes energy costs for aluminium screwcap production will rise, with "knock-on effects" expected imminently.

**Why it is a signal:** Supply chain resilience is now a strategic priority, not an operational detail. Energy-intensive production (closures, glass, transport) faces unpredictable cost pressures.

**Societal layer:** Control

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Diversify packaging suppliers and consider alternative formats (cans, pouches, bag-in-box) that may offer cost stability. Build buffer stock for critical components. Communicate transparently with retail partners about potential disruptions.

**What to monitor:** Energy commodity prices; shipping route disruptions; supplier concentration risk in packaging.

---

### SIGNAL 8: The Packaging Sustainability Multiplier

**What happened:** Tutiac data shows a single SKU closure change prevented 2.6 tonnes of plastic from reaching oceans annually. Scaled across a typical UK supermarket's 700 wine SKUs, potential savings exceed 2,000 tonnes.

**Why it is a signal:** Sustainability gains are achievable through incremental, scalable changes rather than radical system overhauls. Retailers can demonstrate impact through supplier requirements.

**Societal layer:** Control / Identity

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Packaging sustainability is a competitive differentiator and potential barrier to entry. Invest in plant-based closures, recyclable capsules, and lightweight formats. Quantify and communicate environmental impact per SKU.

**What to monitor:** Retailer sustainability requirements; consumer willingness-to-pay for sustainable packaging; competitor packaging innovations.

---

### SIGNAL 9: Restaurant-to-Retail Channel Expansion

**What happened:** Côte Brasserie launches "Côte Uncorked," a French-only online wine shop with 200+ bottles, responding to customer requests to purchase wines enjoyed in restaurants. This follows their existing "Côte At Home" service.

**Why it is a signal:** Hospitality brands are leveraging customer trust and taste discovery to capture retail occasions. The restaurant becomes a showroom; the website becomes the store.

**Societal layer:** Status / Pleasure

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** NA brands should pursue restaurant partnerships that include retail conversion pathways. QR codes on menus linking to purchase pages. Exclusive restaurant-first launches that later expand to retail.

**What to monitor:** Restaurant group e-commerce initiatives; hospitality-to-retail conversion rates; exclusive product partnerships.

---

### SIGNAL 10: The Functional Shot Ritual

**What happened:** TikTok and Instagram wellness influencers promote olive oil and lemon juice shots for "glowing skin and better digestion," despite dubious detox claims.

**Why it is a signal:** Consumers are actively seeking daily rituals with perceived functional benefits. The format (shot), timing (morning), and claimed benefit (beauty/digestion) create a template for habit formation.

**Societal layer:** Control / Pleasure / Spirituality

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Functional NA shots (adaptogens, nootropics, gut health) can capture this ritual-seeking behaviour. Emphasise sensory experience alongside functional claims. Morning and pre-meal occasions are underexplored.

**What to monitor:** Functional shot brand launches; influencer wellness ritual content; morning beverage occasion research.

## TOP 3 EXPERIMENTS

### Experiment 1: The Sommelier's NA Flight

**Hypothesis:** Positioning non-alcoholic beverages as part of an expanded repertoire (alongside rare wines and craft spirits) will increase trial among sophisticated drinkers who currently view NA as a compromise.

**Method:** Partner with three independent wine bars known for adventurous lists. Create a "Curiosity Flight" featuring one natural wine, one obscure spirit, and one premium NA beverage, presented with equal ceremony and tasting notes. Train staff to discuss NA option with same expertise as alcoholic selections. Run for 8 weeks.

**Success metric:** NA beverage trial rate among customers ordering the flight; repeat NA purchases on subsequent visits; qualitative feedback on perceived quality parity.

---

### Experiment 2: Sauna Circuit Hydration Partnership

**Hypothesis:** Post-sauna occasions represent an untapped channel for functional NA beverages, where consumers are primed for hydration and ritual.

**Method:** Partner with five UK sauna venues (mix of urban and coastal) to develop an exclusive "Recovery Ritual" menu featuring electrolyte-enhanced NA options, adaptogenic coolers, and botanical infusions. Include branded glassware and staff training on benefits. Track sales velocity and customer feedback over 12 weeks.

**Success metric:** Revenue per sauna session; customer Net Promoter Score for beverage offering; repeat purchase rate; social media mentions of the ritual.

---

### Experiment 3: The Grown-Up Flavour Drop

**Hypothesis:** Limited-edition, chef-collaborated flavours will drive trial among younger consumers who want NA options that feel culturally relevant and exclusive.

**Method:** Partner with a recognisable chef or mixologist to develop a limited-edition NA beverage flavour (e.g., yuzu-shiso, burnt honey-cardamom). Launch exclusively through a single restaurant group for 4 weeks, then expand to specialty retail. Use scarcity messaging and behind-the-scenes content showing collaboration process.

**Success metric:** Sell-through rate during exclusivity window; social media engagement and earned media coverage; conversion to core product line after limited edition ends.
