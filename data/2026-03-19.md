## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Today's cultural landscape reveals an industry at an inflection point—where the pursuit of quality, authenticity, and intentionality is overtaking the volume-driven growth models of the past decade. The drinks industry, from fine wine to functional beverages, is recalibrating around a central truth: consumers are making fewer, more deliberate choices, and they expect those choices to deliver genuine value—whether that's provenance, taste, or alignment with personal identity.

Three interconnected forces are shaping this moment. First, **premiumisation with purpose**: producers from Rioja to Provence are investing in fewer, better products rather than proliferating SKUs, while auction houses report collectors paying significant premiums for verified provenance. Second, **the democratisation of expertise**: AI-powered personalisation is reducing the cognitive burden of choice, while Gen Z consumers in China are being cultivated as wine ambassadors—signalling a shift from gatekeeping to guided discovery. Third, **the blurring of category boundaries**: bone broth cocktails, dealcoholised Italian wines, and soft play centres with wine bars all point to a culture where traditional category definitions are dissolving in favour of occasion-based consumption.

For non-alcoholic beverages, the strategic imperative is clear: stop competing on novelty and start competing on relevance. The data shows 67% of UK soft drink users would choose an innovative soft drink over a basic no/lo alternative—flavour and experience trump health messaging alone. Meanwhile, bartenders are stripping back complexity, seeking "primary products" they can build relationships with.

The opportunity lies in creating products and experiences that earn their place in a more selective consumer repertoire—through taste, story, and genuine utility. The brands that win will be those that reduce friction while increasing meaning.

## TREND SIGNALS

### SIGNAL 1: Quality Over Quantity in Product Development

**What happened:** At ProWein 2026, major producers like González Byass chose to refresh existing brands rather than launch new SKUs, while Château Puech-Haut entered the no/lo category only after persistent consumer demand and with deliberate restraint to maintain quality parity with flagship wines.

**Why it is a signal:** This represents a fundamental shift from growth-through-proliferation to growth-through-depth. Producers are recognising that oversaturated markets reward brand equity over shelf presence.

**Societal layer:** Status / Control

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** NA brands should resist the temptation to chase every trend with new launches. Instead, invest in perfecting core offerings and building the narrative infrastructure that justifies premium positioning.

**What to monitor:** Trade show launch volumes year-over-year; producer commentary on innovation strategy; retailer feedback on SKU rationalisation.

---

### SIGNAL 2: AI as Cognitive Load Reducer

**What happened:** Virgin Wines reported that AI-powered personalisation is driving measurable increases in purchase completion and trading up, by presenting curated selections aligned to individual taste profiles rather than overwhelming choice.

**Why it is a signal:** This marks the transition of AI from novelty to utility—specifically as a tool for reducing decision fatigue in complex categories. The value proposition is confidence, not just convenience.

**Societal layer:** Control / Pleasure

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Develop recommendation engines or guided discovery experiences that help consumers navigate the expanding NA category with confidence. The goal is to make choosing feel like expertise, not guesswork.

**What to monitor:** Adoption rates of AI recommendation tools in beverage retail; consumer sentiment around "decision confidence"; conversion metrics tied to personalisation.

---

### SIGNAL 3: Bartender Simplification Movement

**What happened:** At ProWein, bartenders signalled a clear preference for simpler drinks over complex, trend-driven serves, with Rotterdam's Rumah Bar emphasising that "primary products are more important than the cocktail itself" and seeking long-term brand partnerships over transactional listings.

**Why it is a signal:** The on-trade is rejecting complexity fatigue. Bartenders want fewer, better products they can build genuine relationships with—a shift from novelty-seeking to craft stewardship.

**Societal layer:** Rebellion / Status

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** NA brands must earn bartender advocacy through product quality and partnership investment, not just marketing spend. Develop trade programmes focused on education and co-creation rather than incentive-driven placement.

**What to monitor:** Cocktail menu complexity metrics; bartender social media discourse; brand partnership longevity in key accounts.

---

### SIGNAL 4: Umami and Functional Cocktails Converge

**What happened:** Bone broth cocktails are gaining traction in US bars, riding the intersection of wellness culture, protein obsession, and the broader umami-forward cocktail trend. The movement has viral momentum on TikTok.

**Why it is a signal:** This represents the collision of functional nutrition with mixology—consumers seeking drinks that deliver both pleasure and perceived physiological benefit. It also signals growing acceptance of savoury profiles in traditionally sweet-dominated categories.

**Societal layer:** Pleasure / Mental Health

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Explore savoury, umami-rich NA formulations that deliver functional benefits (protein, collagen, gut health). Position these as sophisticated alternatives to sweet-forward wellness drinks.

**What to monitor:** TikTok engagement on functional cocktail content; menu adoption in wellness-forward venues; consumer research on savoury beverage acceptance.

---

### SIGNAL 5: Italy Reverses Dealcoholisation Ban

**What happened:** Following a late-2025 decree, Italian producers expect to begin legally dealcoholising wines domestically "any day now," eliminating the cost and carbon footprint of shipping wines abroad for processing.

**Why it is a signal:** A major wine-producing nation legitimising dealcoholisation signals mainstream acceptance of the category. The sustainability angle adds a new dimension to the no/lo value proposition.

**Societal layer:** Control / Status

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Italian dealcoholised wines will enter the market with strong provenance credentials. NA brands should anticipate increased competition from traditional wine regions and consider how to differentiate on origin story and production transparency.

**What to monitor:** Italian dealcoholised wine launch volumes; pricing strategies; export market penetration; sustainability messaging adoption.

---

### SIGNAL 6: Gen Z as Flavour-First Consumers

**What happened:** Mintel research presented at ProWein revealed that taste (47%) and refreshment (42%) are the leading factors influencing Gen Z drink choices—not health. Additionally, 67% of UK soft drink users would choose an innovative soft drink over a basic no/lo alternative.

**Why it is a signal:** The narrative that Gen Z is purely health-motivated is incomplete. Flavour innovation and sensory experience remain primary drivers, suggesting NA brands have been over-indexing on wellness messaging at the expense of taste.

**Societal layer:** Pleasure / Identity

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Rebalance marketing to lead with flavour and sensory experience, with health benefits as supporting rather than primary messaging. Invest in genuinely innovative taste profiles that compete with soft drinks on excitement.

**What to monitor:** Gen Z beverage purchase drivers in ongoing consumer research; social media sentiment analysis on NA taste experiences; competitive innovation from soft drink brands.

---

### SIGNAL 7: Provenance Premium Intensifies

**What happened:** Christie's reports that provenance remains the top driver of wine and spirits auctions, with collectors paying significant premiums for verified origin and storage history. Single-owner sales like the William I. Koch cellar achieved $28.8 million.

**Why it is a signal:** In uncertain markets, authenticity and traceability command premium pricing. This applies beyond fine wine to any category where origin story can be verified and communicated.

**Societal layer:** Status / Control

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Develop robust provenance narratives for NA products—ingredient sourcing, production methods, maker stories. Consider blockchain or other verification technologies for premium offerings.

**What to monitor:** Auction results for authenticated vs. non-authenticated lots; consumer research on provenance importance; adoption of traceability technologies in beverage.

---

### SIGNAL 8: Venue Boundary Dissolution

**What happened:** Debate reignited around soft play centres serving alcohol to parents, with venues like Hampstead's Hullabaloo offering wine bars alongside children's play areas. The discussion reveals ongoing tension around occasion-appropriate consumption.

**Why it is a signal:** Traditional venue categories are dissolving as operators seek to serve multiple needs simultaneously. This creates both opportunity and controversy around where and when alcohol (and by extension, NA alternatives) is appropriate.

**Societal layer:** Belonging / Control

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** NA beverages are uniquely positioned for these hybrid venues—offering adult sophistication without the controversy of alcohol around children. Develop targeted offerings for family-occasion venues.

**What to monitor:** Licensing debates and regulatory changes; hybrid venue proliferation; parent sentiment research on venue alcohol policies.

---

### SIGNAL 9: Algorithm-Driven Social Media Harms Mental Health

**What happened:** The World Happiness Report found that algorithm-driven platforms like Instagram and TikTok are worse for mental health than connection-focused platforms like WhatsApp, with impacts most severe among young people in English-speaking countries.

**Why it is a signal:** Growing evidence of social media harm will accelerate regulatory pressure and consumer backlash against algorithm-driven marketing. Brands reliant on these platforms face reputational and operational risk.

**Societal layer:** Mental Health / Community

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Diversify marketing beyond algorithm-driven platforms. Invest in community-building, owned channels, and real-world experiences that align with consumer desire for genuine connection over performative consumption.

**What to monitor:** Platform usage trends among target demographics; regulatory developments on algorithmic content; brand sentiment tied to platform association.

---

### SIGNAL 10: Wines of Hungary Targets Gen Z Ambassadors

**What happened:** Wines of Hungary is training 10 ambassadors to engage Chinese university students through tastings and workshops, explicitly targeting Gen Z as "highly open-minded" with strong cultures of "hosting, sharing and recommending experiences within their networks."

**Why it is a signal:** Wine regions are investing in peer-to-peer influence rather than traditional trade marketing, recognising that Gen Z discovery happens through social networks rather than expert gatekeepers.

**Societal layer:** Belonging / Identity

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Develop ambassador programmes that empower young consumers to become advocates within their social networks. Focus on experiential education rather than top-down messaging.

**What to monitor:** Ambassador programme effectiveness metrics; Gen Z wine/NA adoption in China; peer recommendation patterns in beverage discovery.

---

### SIGNAL 11: Sotheby's Retail Pivot for Market Testing

**What happened:** Sotheby's Hong Kong is launching a retail space to attract new clients, conduct qualitative market research, and test demand for products not suitable for auction—explicitly using retail as a lower-risk innovation channel.

**Why it is a signal:** Even luxury institutions are adopting lean startup methodologies—using retail as a testing ground before committing to larger investments. This reflects broader uncertainty about consumer preferences.

**Societal layer:** Status / Control

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Consider pop-up retail or limited releases as market testing mechanisms before full launches. Use direct consumer feedback to refine offerings before scale investment.

**What to monitor:** Luxury brand retail experimentation; consumer feedback loop integration; pop-up performance metrics.

## TOP 3 EXPERIMENTS

### Experiment 1: Savoury NA Cocktail Menu Collaboration

**Hypothesis:** Bartenders seeking simpler, quality-focused serves will embrace a savoury NA base spirit designed for umami-forward cocktails, driving menu adoption and consumer trial in premium on-trade venues.

**Method:** Partner with 5-10 progressive cocktail bars to co-develop a limited savoury NA cocktail menu using a new umami-rich base product (incorporating ingredients like mushroom, miso, or fermented botanicals). Provide bartender education on flavour pairing and allow creative freedom in serve development. Run for 8 weeks with dedicated menu placement.

**Success metric:** Menu feature rate >80% of partner venues; consumer reorder rate >25%; bartender Net Promoter Score >50; social media mentions with positive sentiment >70%.

---

### Experiment 2: AI-Guided NA Discovery Platform

**Hypothesis:** Reducing cognitive load through personalised NA recommendations will increase purchase confidence, basket size, and repeat purchase rates among consumers overwhelmed by category proliferation.

**Method:** Develop a simple taste-profiling quiz (5 questions) integrated into e-commerce or retail partner platforms that generates personalised NA recommendations based on flavour preferences, occasion, and past purchases. A/B test against standard category browsing. Run for 12 weeks across 3 retail partners.

**Success metric:** Conversion rate increase >15% vs. control; average order value increase >10%; repeat purchase rate increase >20%; customer satisfaction score on "confidence in choice" >4.2/5.

---

### Experiment 3: Family Venue NA Premium Positioning

**Hypothesis:** Parents in hybrid family/adult venues will pay premium prices for sophisticated NA options that signal adult identity without the controversy of alcohol consumption around children.

**Method:** Partner with 3 family-oriented venues (soft play, family restaurants, resort pools) to introduce a curated NA menu positioned as "adult refreshment"—premium pricing, elegant glassware, menu language emphasising sophistication. Compare sales velocity and margin against standard soft drink offerings over 10 weeks.

**Success metric:** NA menu revenue per cover >2x standard soft drinks; consumer willingness to pay premium >£2 above soft drink baseline; venue operator satisfaction score >4/5; zero negative social media sentiment related to appropriateness.
