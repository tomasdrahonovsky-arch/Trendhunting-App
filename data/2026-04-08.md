## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Today's cultural landscape reveals a profound tension between heritage and reinvention, as industries grapple with how to honour tradition while meeting radically shifted consumer expectations. The drinks sector is experiencing a bifurcation: premium positioning through provenance and craft storytelling on one side, and accessibility-driven democratisation on the other. Port wine's cocktail renaissance, Japanese gin's cultural bridge-building, and the chilled red wine movement in Hong Kong all signal consumers rejecting rigid category rules in favour of personal expression and contextual appropriateness.

The hospitality sector is responding to what might be termed "earned intimacy" — smaller guest counts, hyper-curated experiences, and price points that demand theatrical justification. Koan's £2,000 wine pairing and Drappier's 16-year egg-aged Champagne represent the extreme end of this spectrum, where scarcity and narrative become the product itself.

Meanwhile, structural pressures are reshaping who participates in drinking culture. UK rosé's year-round growth amid declining red and white sales suggests consumers are choosing mood-appropriate options over traditional category loyalty. The American whiskey export collapse (-19%) and UK Champagne's volume-over-value recovery indicate a market recalibrating expectations downward while still seeking moments of elevation.

For non-alcoholic beverages, the opportunity lies in the space between these extremes: products that deliver the storytelling depth and sensory sophistication of premium alcohol without the barriers of intoxication, price, or occasion-specificity. The cultural permission to break rules — chilling reds, mixing Port, drinking rosé in December — extends naturally to choosing sophisticated non-alcoholic options without apology. The moment favours brands that understand beverage choice as identity performance, not mere refreshment.

## TREND SIGNALS

### SIGNAL 1: The Cocktail Rehabilitation of "Stuffy" Categories

**What happened:** Graham's Port is running its fifth annual cocktail competition specifically designed to combat Port's image as "stuffy, dated and solely a festive serve," training bartenders to become "ambassadors for life" and reviving historical serves like the Ruby and Lemon.

**Why it is a signal:** Legacy beverage categories are actively investing in format disruption to reach younger consumers. The explicit goal of creating lifelong bartender advocates suggests recognition that gatekeepers, not advertising, drive category rehabilitation.

**Societal layer:** Rebellion / Status

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Non-alcoholic brands with heritage ingredients (botanicals, fermented bases, traditional preparations) can position as the "rediscovered classic" — leveraging historical legitimacy while offering contemporary relevance. Partner with bartenders as co-creators rather than mere pourers.

**What to monitor:** Bartender competition entries featuring non-alcoholic bases; cocktail menu sections dedicated to "forgotten serves" reimagined without alcohol.

---

### SIGNAL 2: Taste as Self-Expression, Not Consensus

**What happened:** Robert Pattinson's 1664 campaign explicitly positions taste as subjective and personal, with Carlsberg's VP stating the brand "celebrates those confident enough to trust their own taste" and embraces "the clash of opinions."

**Why it is a signal:** Major beverage brands are abandoning universal quality claims in favour of validating individual preference. This represents a shift from "this is objectively good" to "your choice reveals who you are."

**Societal layer:** Identity / Belonging

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Stop defending non-alcoholic choices as "just as good as" alcohol. Instead, position choosing NA as a confident taste statement — an expression of discernment rather than deprivation. Marketing should celebrate the chooser, not justify the choice.

**What to monitor:** Social media sentiment around beverage choice as identity marker; influencer content framing drink selection as personality expression.

### SIGNAL 3: Climate-Driven Format Innovation

**What happened:** Hong Kong sommeliers report significant uptick in chilled red wine requests as temperatures rise, with venues like Crushed Wine Bar seeing customers "actively asking for it" rather than needing education. Winemaking is adapting with "lighter extraction, minimal new oak and shorter ageing."

**Why it is a signal:** Climate change is directly reshaping consumption formats and production methods. Consumers are granting themselves permission to break temperature rules based on context rather than convention.

**Societal layer:** Pleasure / Control

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Develop temperature-flexible NA products and serving recommendations. Create "climate-responsive" menus that shift offerings based on weather. The permission to serve things "wrong" opens space for unconventional NA presentations.

**What to monitor:** Menu innovations tied to weather/season; consumer willingness to accept non-traditional serving temperatures; climate adaptation in production messaging.

### SIGNAL 4: The Progression Narrative Over Fusion

**What happened:** Thai chef Pam Soontornyanakij explicitly rejects "fusion" framing, describing her Thai-Chinese cuisine as "progression" — respecting roots while allowing cuisine to move forward. She emphasises that cultures "have already lived together for generations."

**Why it is a signal:** Authenticity discourse is evolving from purity to continuity. The new legitimacy comes not from unchanged tradition but from demonstrable lineage and respectful evolution.

**Societal layer:** Identity / Belonging

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Frame innovation as "progression" rather than disruption. NA beverages drawing on fermentation traditions, botanical heritage, or regional ingredients can claim legitimacy through lineage rather than novelty. Avoid "fusion" language; emphasise natural evolution.

**What to monitor:** Consumer response to heritage-forward vs. innovation-forward positioning; success of brands emphasising generational craft knowledge.

### SIGNAL 5: Japanese Provenance as Quality Shorthand

**What happened:** Ukiyo Japanese gin reports 40% CAGR despite overall gin category decline, with distributor noting Japan provides "immediate quality cues" and functions as "almost a passport" to aspiration. The brand specifically targets "accessible premium" positioning.

**Why it is a signal:** Geographic provenance is functioning as a trust mechanism in categories where consumers lack expertise to evaluate quality directly. Japan specifically signals precision, care, and elevated craft.

**Societal layer:** Status / Control

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Japanese-origin or Japanese-inspired NA beverages have inherent positioning advantage. Consider partnerships, ingredients, or production techniques that legitimately connect to Japanese craft traditions. The "accessible premium" price point (trade-up without strain) is the sweet spot.

**What to monitor:** Premium NA launches with geographic provenance claims; consumer willingness to pay premiums based on origin; travel retail performance of provenance-led brands.

### SIGNAL 6: Rosé's Seasonal Liberation

**What happened:** UK rosé sales grew 3% in volume while red (-6%) and white (-4%) declined. Critically, winter rosé sales rose 1%, with M&S reporting Christmas rosé sales "nearly double those in the hottest week of July."

**Why it is a signal:** A category previously confined to summer occasions has achieved year-round relevance, suggesting consumers are rejecting seasonal beverage rules entirely. The "permission" to drink rosé in December opens permission structures broadly.

**Societal layer:** Pleasure / Rebellion

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Challenge seasonal assumptions about NA beverages. Products positioned as "summer refreshers" may be artificially limiting their market. Develop year-round occasion strategies and winter-appropriate NA options with the same approachability as rosé.

**What to monitor:** Seasonal sales patterns for NA categories; success of counter-seasonal marketing; consumer language around "appropriate" occasions.

### SIGNAL 7: The Intimacy Premium

**What happened:** Copenhagen's Koan serves 23 guests per service with an 18-course menu and £2,000 wine pairing featuring Krug Clos du Mesnil by the glass. The restaurant earned two Michelin stars within ten weeks of opening.

**Why it is a signal:** Ultra-premium hospitality is scaling down, not up. Exclusivity is achieved through constraint rather than expansion, with price functioning as access control and intimacy as the luxury itself.

**Societal layer:** Status / Belonging

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Develop NA offerings specifically for ultra-premium contexts where alcohol abstention shouldn't mean experience diminishment. Create "collector's edition" NA products with genuine scarcity. The intimate dining boom requires sophisticated NA pairings that justify their place on £2,000 menus.

**What to monitor:** Michelin-level restaurants' NA pairing programmes; willingness of high-net-worth consumers to pay premium for NA options; emergence of "sommelier-selected" NA categories.

### SIGNAL 8: Direct-to-Consumer as Category Survival Strategy

**What happened:** US craft breweries show "overwhelming support" (63% of legal-age Americans, 81% of craft beer drinkers) for expanded direct-to-consumer shipping laws, currently legal in only 12 states. This emerges amid brewery closures and 16 million barrels of excess Kentucky whiskey stock.

**Why it is a signal:** Traditional distribution is failing producers in declining categories. DTC represents both survival mechanism and relationship-building opportunity, bypassing gatekeepers who may deprioritise struggling categories.

**Societal layer:** Control / Community

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** NA brands should aggressively build DTC capabilities now, before category maturation makes customer acquisition expensive. The regulatory advantages NA beverages hold over alcohol in shipping should be leveraged as competitive moat.

**What to monitor:** DTC shipping regulation changes; craft beverage subscription model performance; customer lifetime value comparisons between DTC and retail channels.

### SIGNAL 9: The Friendship Grief Recognition

**What happened:** Dazed explores why friendship breakups hurt as intensely as romantic ones, with the author describing being unable to approach a former friend at King's Cross despite 18 months of separation, acknowledging "too much history and pain."

**Why it is a signal:** Cultural discourse is finally validating non-romantic relationship loss as legitimate grief. This expands the emotional landscape beverages can address — not just romantic celebration or commiseration, but the full spectrum of human connection and its dissolution.

**Societal layer:** Mental health / Community / Belonging

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Create spaces and products for friendship maintenance and repair, not just romantic occasions. The "catch-up drink" occasion is underserved by current NA positioning. Consider how venues can facilitate reconnection without alcohol's social lubricant.

**What to monitor:** Hospitality concepts designed around friendship (not dating); beverage occasions framed around platonic connection; social media discourse on friendship maintenance rituals.

### SIGNAL 10: The 16-Year Patience Play

**What happened:** Champagne Drappier unveiled Éclose 2012, aged in egg-shaped oak barrels since 2010 under the codename "Ovum." The project was kept secret for nearly 16 years, with the family noting "it simply wasn't ready" when they considered releasing it earlier.

**Why it is a signal:** In an era of rapid product cycles, extreme patience is becoming a differentiator. The willingness to wait — and to keep waiting until quality demands release — signals a counter-cultural commitment that commands premium positioning.

**Societal layer:** Status / Spirituality

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Develop genuinely aged NA products where time creates complexity (fermented, barrel-rested, or otherwise transformed). The story of patience is as valuable as the liquid itself. Consider "vault" programmes where products are held until optimal.

**What to monitor:** Consumer response to age statements on NA products; willingness to pay for demonstrated patience; success of "when it's ready" release strategies.

### SIGNAL 11: The Musician Hotness Phenomenon

**What happened:** Dazed unpacks why musicians possess unique attractiveness, using Geese frontman Cameron Winter as exemplar of "wait, am I attracted to him?" appeal, noting this arrives "in a cultural moment when men are thinking about their appearance more than ever."

**Why it is a signal:** Attractiveness is increasingly tied to creative expression and emotional intelligence rather than conventional physical attributes. The "interesting over handsome" shift has implications for brand personality and spokesperson selection.

**Societal layer:** Identity / Pleasure / Status

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Partner with musicians and creatives rather than conventional celebrities or athletes. The emotional resonance of artistic expression aligns with NA beverages' need to convey depth and sophistication without relying on intoxication narratives.

**What to monitor:** Brand partnership announcements in beverage sector; consumer response to creative vs. athletic endorsements; music venue and festival NA offerings.

## TOP 3 EXPERIMENTS

### Experiment 1: The Progression Menu

**Hypothesis:** Consumers will pay premium prices for NA beverages positioned as "progressions" of traditional drinks categories (the way Thai-Chinese cuisine is positioned as progression, not fusion), with explicit lineage storytelling connecting innovation to heritage.

**Method:** Partner with three high-end restaurants to develop NA pairing menus where each beverage is presented with a "progression card" explaining its heritage connection (e.g., a sophisticated NA aperitif positioned as the progression of vermouth traditions, or a complex botanical drink as the progression of digestif culture). Compare sales, satisfaction scores, and willingness-to-pay against standard NA pairing menus without progression framing.

**Success metric:** 25% higher average spend on NA pairings with progression positioning; qualitative feedback indicating guests perceive NA options as "legitimate" rather than "substitute."

---

### Experiment 2: The Counter-Seasonal NA Launch

**Hypothesis:** Launching a sophisticated NA beverage in its "wrong" season (a refreshing, light NA in December; a warming, complex NA in July) will generate disproportionate attention and establish the brand as category rule-breaker, following rosé's successful seasonal liberation.

**Method:** Develop two NA products with clear seasonal associations. Launch each in the opposite season with messaging explicitly celebrating the rule-break ("Winter brightness" for the light product; "Summer depth" for the warming one). Track earned media, social conversation, and sales velocity compared to category norms for seasonal launches.

**Success metric:** 3x earned media coverage compared to conventional seasonal launch; sales velocity within 80% of "correct" season launch despite counter-positioning.

---

### Experiment 3: The Bartender Ambassador Programme

**Hypothesis:** Training bartenders as "NA ambassadors for life" (mirroring Graham's Port strategy) will drive sustained on-premise velocity more effectively than consumer marketing, as bartenders become advocates who actively recommend NA options.

**Method:** Recruit 50 bartenders across 10 UK cities for an intensive NA certification programme including production visits, blending workshops, and competition elements. Provide ongoing support and recognition. Track NA sales in participating venues vs. matched control venues over 12 months. Survey bartenders on recommendation behaviour and confidence.

**Success metric:** 40% higher NA category sales in participating venues; 70% of trained bartenders reporting they "actively recommend" NA options to guests who haven't requested them.
