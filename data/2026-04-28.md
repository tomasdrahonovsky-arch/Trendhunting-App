## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Today's cultural landscape reveals a drinks industry in profound transition—not merely adapting to market pressures, but fundamentally reconsidering what beverages mean in people's lives. The convergence of climate strain, geopolitical disruption, and shifting consumer identity is forcing a reckoning across categories.

France's decision to uproot 28,000 hectares of vineyards signals more than oversupply correction—it marks the end of production-first thinking in traditional beverage categories. Simultaneously, Carlsberg's rosé beer launch demonstrates how category boundaries are dissolving as consumers increasingly choose drinks based on occasion and identity rather than product taxonomy. The "lifestyle-led proposition" language is telling: beverages are becoming extensions of self-expression rather than functional choices.

Three deeper currents deserve attention. First, the sustainability-luxury convergence is accelerating. Fukuju's organic sake and Puklavec's carbon-neutral wine aren't niche experiments—they represent a new baseline expectation where environmental credentials enhance rather than compromise premium positioning. Second, independent producers across beer and wine are fighting for visibility against global consolidation, with the IBE's "magician's choice" metaphor capturing consumer frustration with manufactured authenticity. Third, the Iran conflict's ripple effects on agricultural inputs remind us that beverage production remains vulnerable to geopolitical shocks in ways that demand supply chain resilience.

For non-alcoholic beverage builders, the strategic imperative is clear: the same forces reshaping wine and beer—premiumisation, sustainability, identity expression, and category fluidity—are creating unprecedented opportunity. Consumers seeking "lifestyle-led" drinking experiences are increasingly agnostic about alcohol content. The question isn't whether non-alcoholic can compete, but whether brands can deliver the emotional and social value that drinkers now expect from every beverage occasion.

## TREND SIGNALS

### SIGNAL 1: Category Boundaries Dissolve as Lifestyle Trumps Liquid

**What happened:** Carlsberg Britvic launched 1664 Rosé, a berry-flavoured beer with a "subtle pink hue," explicitly designed to compete in wine occasions like alfresco dining and elevated social gatherings. The brand describes it as a "lifestyle-led proposition" that "blurs boundaries between categories."

**Why it is a signal:** This represents a fundamental shift from product-category thinking to occasion-based competition. When a major brewer deliberately positions beer against wine using viniculture language, it signals that consumers increasingly choose drinks based on mood, setting, and identity rather than traditional category loyalty.

**Societal layer:** Identity / Pleasure

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Non-alcoholic brands should stop positioning against alcoholic equivalents and instead compete for occasions. The opportunity lies in owning specific moments—the aperitivo hour, the dinner party, the celebration—rather than being "alcohol-free wine" or "non-alcoholic beer."

**What to monitor:** Cross-category product launches from major beverage companies; consumer language shifts from "I'll have a beer" to "I want something light/refreshing/sophisticated"; occasion-based merchandising in retail and on-premise.

### SIGNAL 2: Sustainability Becomes Premium Credential, Not Compromise

**What happened:** Japanese sake producer Fukuju released an organic, carbon-neutral Junmai Daiginjo using rare organic Yamada-Nishiki rice, positioning it as "sustainable luxury." Separately, Slovenian winemaker Puklavec launched Trois Femmes in carbon-neutral bottles, targeting fine dining through JRE-affiliated restaurants.

**Why it is a signal:** The framing has inverted. Sustainability is no longer positioned as sacrifice or ethical obligation—it's being marketed as a marker of quality and exclusivity. Organic Yamada-Nishiki rice is rare precisely because sustainable production is difficult, making it a scarcity signal rather than a compromise.

**Societal layer:** Status / Spirituality

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Premium non-alcoholic brands should lead with sustainability as a quality indicator, not a secondary benefit. The narrative should be: "This is better because it's sustainable," not "This is sustainable and also good."

**What to monitor:** Luxury hospitality adoption of sustainability-forward beverage programmes; consumer willingness to pay premiums for verified environmental credentials; emergence of sustainability certifications as status markers.

### SIGNAL 3: The Authenticity Crisis in Independent Beverages

**What happened:** The Independent Brewers of Europe reported that 43% of independent brewers cite survival as their 2026 priority, with global brewer dominance their top challenge. IBE chairman Barry Watts described consumers facing a "magician's choice at the bar—where what looks like a range of local beers is in fact mass produced by the same global company."

**Why it is a signal:** Consumer desire for authenticity is being systematically exploited through pseudo-craft branding. The proliferation of "indie beer" certification schemes across Europe (Italy's Marchio Indipendente Artigianale, Austria's 100% unabhängig, UK's Indie Beer logo) indicates both the problem's severity and consumers' hunger for genuine provenance.

**Societal layer:** Belonging / Rebellion

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Independent non-alcoholic producers have a structural advantage—the category is young enough that genuine craft credentials are still available. Brands should invest heavily in provenance storytelling and consider collective certification schemes before large players dominate the narrative.

**What to monitor:** Acquisition activity by major beverage companies in non-alcoholic space; consumer sentiment research on brand ownership awareness; success of indie certification programmes in driving purchase behaviour.

### SIGNAL 4: Vineyard Contraction Signals Demand-Side Reckoning

**What happened:** France is removing 28,000 hectares of vineyards under a €130 million state programme, with 5,800 growers applying for support. 37% will exit wine production entirely. The majority of removals involve red grape varieties, reflecting weakening demand.

**Why it is a signal:** This isn't cyclical adjustment—it's structural contraction driven by "evolving consumer behaviour and declining demand for red wine." When a wine-producing nation pays farmers to stop growing grapes, it acknowledges that production capacity has permanently outstripped demand. The specific weakness in red wine suggests broader shifts in drinking preferences toward lighter, more refreshing options.

**Societal layer:** Control / Identity

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** The decline of red wine creates specific whitespace. Non-alcoholic brands should study what red wine provided—complexity, food pairing, evening ritual—and develop products that deliver these benefits without the heaviness or alcohol that consumers are rejecting.

**What to monitor:** Category-specific consumption data (red vs. white vs. rosé vs. sparkling); age cohort preferences in wine; emergence of "red wine alternatives" in non-alcoholic space.

### SIGNAL 5: Geopolitical Fragility Exposes Agricultural Vulnerability

**What happened:** The Iran conflict has closed the Strait of Hormuz, driving crude oil prices from under $70 to $119 and increasing agricultural input costs dramatically. Urea (primary nitrogen source for vineyards) is up 50% since February; sulfur supplies are constrained as the Gulf accounts for 44% of globally traded seaborne sulfur.

**Why it is a signal:** Beverage production's dependence on global supply chains for basic agricultural inputs is being stress-tested. The article notes producers are "accelerating efforts to build more resilient and environmentally sustainable business models"—sustainability is becoming a risk management strategy, not just a marketing position.

**Societal layer:** Control

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Supply chain resilience should be a strategic priority, not an operational afterthought. Brands with localised, simplified ingredient sourcing will have competitive advantages during disruption. This also strengthens the case for sustainability investments that reduce input dependencies.

**What to monitor:** Agricultural commodity prices; regional sourcing initiatives; consumer awareness of supply chain issues; brand communication around ingredient provenance and security.

### SIGNAL 6: Women Reclaiming Winemaking Narrative

**What happened:** Multiple articles highlighted women-led winemaking initiatives: Puklavec's Trois Femmes created by female sommeliers across Europe; California's Wine Institute profiling Aly and Niki Wente as "shaping the new California"; Uruguay's Emilia Barabino leading Garzón's Asia-Pacific strategy.

**Why it is a signal:** The industry is actively constructing new narratives around female leadership, moving beyond tokenism toward substantive positioning. The Trois Femmes collaboration—women from Slovenia, Germany, and Netherlands working together—suggests a pan-European network forming around gender identity in beverage production.

**Societal layer:** Identity / Belonging

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** The non-alcoholic category, being newer and less encumbered by traditional hierarchies, has an opportunity to be genuinely female-led rather than retrofitting diversity narratives. Brands should consider whether their founding teams, production leadership, and brand voices authentically represent the consumers they're targeting.

**What to monitor:** Female founder/leadership representation in non-alcoholic brands; consumer response to gender-focused brand narratives; emergence of women-focused beverage communities and networks.

### SIGNAL 7: Alcohol Harm Awareness Intensifies in Emerging Markets

**What happened:** Karnataka, India reported alcohol-related harm costing ₹51,000 crore (£4.8bn) annually, representing 1.4-2.6% of state GDP. The government is moving to tax alcohol based on ABV, and the report highlights domestic violence, road accidents, and healthcare burden from a relatively small group of heavy drinkers.

**Why it is a signal:** Emerging markets are developing sophisticated frameworks for understanding alcohol's social costs, potentially presaging regulatory tightening. The ABV-based taxation model could spread, creating structural advantages for lower-alcohol and non-alcoholic options.

**Societal layer:** Control / Mental Health

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Emerging markets may leapfrog Western markets in non-alcoholic adoption if regulatory frameworks incentivise lower-alcohol consumption. Brands should monitor policy developments in India, Southeast Asia, and Africa for early-mover opportunities.

**What to monitor:** Alcohol taxation policy changes globally; public health campaigns in emerging markets; distribution infrastructure development for non-alcoholic options in high-growth regions.

### SIGNAL 8: Kingsland's £65M Bet on No/Low Category

**What happened:** Manchester-based Kingsland Drinks Group secured £65 million in funding, explicitly citing "increased focus on the fast-growing no and low category" alongside its established wine and spirits operations. New MD Sarah Baldwin (former Purity Soft Drinks CEO) signals strategic pivot.

**Why it is a signal:** When a 70-year-old drinks company with deep wine and spirits expertise makes a major capital commitment to no/low, it validates the category's commercial maturity. The appointment of a soft drinks executive to lead suggests they see the category through a different lens than traditional alcohol producers.

**Societal layer:** Status

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** Established drinks infrastructure is pivoting toward non-alcoholic, bringing capital, distribution, and retail relationships. Independent non-alcoholic brands should consider whether partnership or competition is the right strategy as category consolidation accelerates.

**What to monitor:** M&A activity in non-alcoholic space; traditional drinks company investments in no/low; retail shelf space allocation trends; contract manufacturing capacity for non-alcoholic beverages.

### SIGNAL 9: Postpartum Identity and Intimacy Struggles

**What happened:** Research from Bluebella found 1 in 5 mothers report children impacting their sexual pleasure, with experts citing "touched out" exhaustion, identity shifts, and physical changes. The article frames returning to intimacy as requiring emotional safety and non-sexual connection first.

**Why it is a signal:** While not directly beverage-related, this signals broader conversations about life transitions, identity, and the need for rituals that support wellbeing during challenging periods. Mothers represent a significant demographic often overlooked in beverage marketing beyond "wine mom" tropes.

**Societal layer:** Identity / Mental Health / Pleasure

**Implication for non-alcoholic beverages / hospitality:** There's an underserved opportunity in beverages that support new parents—not through alcohol (which conflicts with breastfeeding and sleep deprivation) but through functional benefits, ritual, and self-care positioning. Evening wind-down drinks, adaptogenic relaxation beverages, or simply sophisticated options that don't require sobriety compromises.

**What to monitor:** Parenting community conversations about alcohol and self-care; functional beverage adoption among new parents; emergence of "fourth trimester" wellness products.

## TOP 3 EXPERIMENTS

### Experiment 1: Occasion-First Product Development

**Hypothesis:** Consumers will respond more strongly to non-alcoholic beverages positioned around specific occasions (e.g., "The Dinner Party Drink," "The Aperitivo Hour") than to category-based positioning (e.g., "non-alcoholic wine alternative").

**Method:** Develop three identical liquid products with different positioning: one as "non-alcoholic sparkling wine," one as "The Host's Pour" (positioned for dinner party hosts), and one as "Golden Hour" (positioned for sunset/aperitivo occasions). Test through A/B landing pages, social content, and limited retail placement. Measure click-through rates, conversion, repeat purchase, and qualitative feedback on purchase motivation.

**Success metric:** Occasion-positioned variants achieve 25%+ higher conversion rates and demonstrate stronger emotional resonance in post-purchase surveys (measured through Net Promoter Score and open-ended feedback analysis).

---

### Experiment 2: Sustainability-as-Luxury Pricing Test

**Hypothesis:** Consumers will pay a 20-30% premium for non-alcoholic beverages with verified, prominent sustainability credentials when positioned as quality indicators rather than ethical obligations.

**Method:** Create two versions of the same premium non-alcoholic product: one with standard sustainability messaging ("We care about the planet") and one with luxury-sustainability positioning ("Rare organic botanicals, carbon-neutral production, limited availability"). Test pricing elasticity across both positioning strategies through direct-to-consumer sales, measuring willingness to pay, perceived quality ratings, and gift purchase rates.

**Success metric:** Luxury-sustainability positioning supports 25%+ price premium without significant volume decline; gift purchase rate increases by 40%+ (indicating status/quality perception).

---

### Experiment 3: New Parent Evening Ritual Product

**Hypothesis:** New parents (particularly mothers) represent an underserved market for sophisticated non-alcoholic evening beverages that support wind-down rituals without the conflicts of alcohol (breastfeeding, sleep quality, next-day energy).

**Method:** Develop a functional relaxation beverage with adaptogens/botanicals, positioned explicitly for the "after bedtime" moment. Partner with 3-5 parenting influencers and 2-3 postpartum wellness practitioners for authentic seeding. Measure through dedicated landing page conversions, subscription sign-up rates, and qualitative research on ritual integration.

**Success metric:** 15%+ of trial customers convert to subscription; qualitative research reveals product integration into established evening routines; customer acquisition cost competitive with or below category benchmarks despite niche targeting.
